# CHARACTER HISTORIES

_Who they were before the story began._

_These profiles contain no spoilers for Man Amongst the Clouds. They describe the lives, appearances, and histories of the principal characters as they exist at or before the opening of the novel._

---

## AELO

### Appearance

Small for fifteen — shorter than every boy his age by a head, and thinner than most by more than that. His body has the proportions of a child who stopped growing at eleven and never received a reason why: narrow shoulders, a ribcage you can count through his shirt, wrists so slim the bones are visible when he turns his hands. His face is angular, fine-boned, with the sharp cheekbones and wide-set eyes that mark the Cloud People but that no one in his village has ever seen enough Cloud People to recognize. His eyes are dark — a grey so deep it reads as black in low light, the kind of eyes that absorb attention rather than reflecting it. His hair is dark, overgrown, falling past his ears.

He moves carefully. Not timidly — carefully, in the way of a person who has learned that his body does not always do what he expects. He walks lightly. He sits with his knees drawn up. He takes up less space than he needs to, as if he has been practicing the art of being small for so long that it has become structural.

On the inside of his left wrist, barely visible in most light, an amber mark. He has had it as long as he can remember. He does not know what it is.

### History

He has none. That is the point of him.

He was raised in a village too small to name, at the edge of the Canopy, by a man who limps and drinks and wraps half his face in linen. He has no family name. No mother. No father. No story of where he came from. He is a boy built from absence — the absence of history, the absence of explanation, the absence of the thing that other children have and that he has filed under the single, heavy word _belonging_.

What he does have: a gentle heart that flinches at suffering. An instinct for the emotional weather of the people around him. A sensitivity so acute that he can feel his caretaker's nightmares through the wall — not the content, just the texture, the heat and terror of someone else's worst memories pressing against his sleep like a hand against glass.

He eats his porridge every morning. The porridge contains something. He knows this the way he knows most of the truths in his life — through the particular taste of a lie. The porridge makes the world quieter. He has never known the world at full volume.

He is fifteen. He is no one. He is waiting for the story he does not know he is in.

## JALO SOTURI

### Appearance — Before

His name was Halvar Eld, and he was built for the work he was sworn to do.

Broad-shouldered, heavy-framed, carrying the dense, functional muscle of a man who had trained since adolescence in both combat and Healing — the Vael Guard's double demand. His jaw was broad, his nose carried a scar across the bridge where a training blade had caught him at seventeen, and his grey-green eyes held the steady, assessing quality of a soldier who was also a Healer: someone trained to read a room for threats and a body for wounds in the same glance. His hands were large, capable, the hands of a man who could raise a wall of stone with a staff and mend a shattered bone with his palm. He wore his hair short, military-cut. He moved with economy — nothing wasted, nothing performed, every gesture the minimum required to accomplish the action.

He was not handsome in the way that court poets meant the word. He was handsome in the way that functional things are handsome — a well-made blade, a load-bearing wall, a bridge that holds. His face was the face of a man you would trust with something precious, and this was, as it turned out, exactly what happened.

### Appearance — After

A ruin of a man, and he prefers it that way.

The broad shoulders remain, but fifteen years of village life and root wine have converted the soldier's muscle into something heavier and less precise — endurance where there was once power. His left knee grinds with every step, producing a rhythm so consistent it functions as a signature: _tap-step-drag, tap-step-drag_. The knee was broken in a fall from a garden wall, carrying a baby in one arm. It healed wrong. He never fixed it — a Healer cannot heal himself, and the pain is his penance.

Half his face is wrapped in stained linen, knotted behind the ear. The visible half is weathered, lined, ordinary — the remnant of the face that was. The hidden half is what he made of it: a terrain of damage, ridged and cratered, the skin pulled tight in some places and hanging loose in others, the brow melted, the lashes gone, the tissue thick with scar that has long since given up trying to remember what it was before. He did this to himself. A heated skinning blade, pressed methodically across the left cheek, around the eye socket, along the jaw — because the face of Halvar Eld was known to every bounty hunter and Mrak Patrol in the kingdom, and the face was a death sentence for the baby, not for him. The eye survived. He was careful about the eye.

His hands tremble now. The tremble is worst in the mornings, before the tincture — a combination of withdrawal, phantom pain from fifteen years of Healing others, and the Mold's slow tax on sensation. His fingertips are going numb. He compensates with grip.

He carries a cane. The cane is dark wood, heavy, carved with marks that glow faintly in certain light. He says the marks are decorative. They are not decorative. Inside the cane is a staff.

### History

He was twenty-three when he spoke the oath. A stone chamber in the Cloud Palace, amber torches, cedar and iron. Twelve men in a line. Halvar third from the left. Captain Sera — silver-haired, who had held the post for thirty years — stood before them and said: _You are the wall between the fire and the song. You will burn before the song goes silent._

He meant every word. Twenty-three and certain that meaning was enough.

His posting was the Cloud Palace. The morning shift — dawn to noon, guarding the garden terrace where Queen Maera sang to her flowers and the infant prince Vero toddled between the beds calling the clouds _puffs_ and calling Halvar _Hal_. King Aldric on the balcony above, leaning on the rail, his face soft with the specific expression of a man watching his wife and son and understanding that this was the thing he would remember when everything else was gone.

Halvar stood at the garden gate a thousand times. He felt the Song move through the stone beneath his feet. He thought: _This is what I am protecting. This morning. This family._

Then the coup came. Varas seized the throne. Aldric and Vero were taken. The Vael Guard was hunted. Three guards attempted the extraction of Maera and her infant son — a second child, born in the shadow of the fall. Two were killed in the first corridor. Halvar made it to the nursery.

Maera was singing. Not a spell. A lullaby — the Song reduced to its smallest form: a mother singing her baby to sleep as the door broke down. She pressed a pouch of herbs into his hand and made him promise. Half a pinch. Warm water. Every morning. It will keep him hidden.

He took the baby. He ran. He heard her die as he crossed the garden wall.

He broke his knee on the descent. He burned his face three days later, at a river in the deep Canopy, with a skinning blade heated over deadwood. The sound was not what he expected. The smell was pork. The pain was a country he would live in for the rest of his life. When it was done, he plunged his face in the river and the current carried the blood downstream, and with it, the last physical evidence that the man kneeling at the bank had ever been anyone other than a nameless trapper with a ruined face and a baby and a story about a dead wife.

Halvar Eld died in that river. Jalo Soturi was born in the same water.

He named the baby. Held him to his chest and whispered it into the dark — a Vael Guard word, a prayer disguised as a name. _Aelo._ A breath of remembering.

He found the village. He built the cottage. He locked the cabinet. He mixed the herbs every morning — half a pinch, chalk and copper, the bell silenced for another day — and he watched the boy eat, and he exhaled, because the promise was kept.

Fifteen years of mornings. Fifteen years of the exhale. Fifteen years of drinking root wine in the evenings — not the reckless consumption of a man who has given up but the precise, medicinal intake of a man who has calculated exactly how much numbness he requires so that his nightmares do not bleed through the wall into the boy who can feel them. Some nights require more. The nights with the smoke and the splintering door and the voice require the most.

He knows the herbs the way a scholar knows texts. He was trained by the Vael Guard's Healers, who understood that a warrior who could also mend was worth ten who could only break. Breedlebuck for inflammation. Fiddleroot for fever. Ironbark for the bone-ache that wakes him at three in the morning, the knee grinding in the dark, the phantom wounds of every person he has ever healed playing their nightly concert in his bones.

He is fifty-three. The oath is the same. The meaning has changed.

Every morning, he unlocks the cabinet. He prepares the tincture. He prepares the porridge. He watches the boy eat. He exhales.

The exhale is the most important moment of his day.

## KING VARAS

### Appearance

No one alive has seen him clearly in over a decade. The descriptions converge on a feeling: _wrong_.

He was tall once. The height has compressed into something denser — large the way a boulder is large, taking up more space than his dimensions account for. His skin has thickened, darkened, taken on a texture that observers describe as bark or stone, depending on the light. His hands are massive — fingers swollen to twice their natural width, knuckles ridged with growths. His eyes are the color of Elder Stone: flat, lightless amber that reflects nothing.

He moves as if underwater. Every gesture is conserved. He is connected to his throne by Blood Vine that runs from the armrests into his forearms, from the seat into his spine. The throne is not furniture. It is a life-support system.

He speaks softly. He is unfailingly polite.

### History

Before he was a king, he was a boy who reached up to be held and watched his father turn away.

A corridor in the Cloud Palace. He was four. He called out. His father did not turn around. The memory is his oldest and his sharpest, and it is the foundation on which everything else was built.

King Aldric was a good man — a Knower who governed by truth and believed magic was a shared resource. On the day his firstborn son was placed in his arms, he Knew the boy and felt nothing. Not evil. Silence. A void where the capacity for communion should have been. His son was born deaf to the only language that mattered.

Aldric recoiled. He did not mean to. But the recoil became favoritism toward his younger son Vero, and the favoritism became the shape of a childhood, and the childhood became a man, and the man became a hunger.

Varas studied the Elder Stones and the Blood Vine. He discovered the Drain. The first time he used it, he felt stolen memory flood into him, and for half a second he felt what everyone else felt — connection, warmth, the sense of being part of the world's conversation. Then it was gone. The absence was worse than not knowing. The worse was the beginning of the addiction.

He seized the throne. He sealed the borders. He burned the histories. He perverted the ceremonies. He hunted the bloodline that carried the Song, because the Song was the one thing he could not steal.

He sits on his throne surrounded by silence, chasing the echo of that first half-second, which will never come again. He tells himself he is a god. He is a deaf man in a concert hall, stealing instruments he cannot play.

## BAARA — The Knife

### Appearance

Tall, lean, constructed for speed. His body is an economy — no excess weight, every muscle maintained at the exact threshold of its utility. He moves like a predator: efficient, no wasted motion. His face is angular — sharp jaw, high cheekbones, a mouth that defaults to a line so straight it appears drawn. His eyes are dark, watchful, giving his resting expression a quality of permanent assessment.

He wears dark armor — layered leather over chainmail, designed for speed. He carries no visible weapon except a single smooth stone in a holster at his belt, worn to a dull sheen by years of handling.

He is twenty-three. He looks older. The aging lives in the eyes.

### History

His first memory is of his father's back. A corridor. He was four. He called out. His father did not turn around.

He was raised by a rotation of castle servants, none of whom stayed long enough to love him. His father visited to assess progress — never to play, never to hold. Baara learned to fight because fighting was the only activity that made his father look at him.

He had killed eleven men before his fifteenth birthday. Most of what came before twelve is fog — his earliest clear memories begin around the age he was given a sword, which is also the age he refused it and requested a stone instead. "A sword is someone else's work," he told his father. "A stone is the world's." His father laughed — the only time Baara can remember his father laughing — and then stopped laughing and looked at him with something that might have been interest. "The Knife," his father said. "Yes. That suits you." He has not been called Baara since.

He keeps a wooden box under his cot. Inside: a blue feather from a bird he saw in the Canopy when he was nine. A smooth river stone from Bahar that fits perfectly in his palm. A piece of sea glass, green as springtime. A dried flower from a crack in the prison floor. A scrap of cloth from a woman who smiled at him once.

He does not know why he keeps these things. If his father found the box, he would call it weakness. Baara thinks his father might be right. He keeps the box anyway.

He has begun to forget things. Small things — the name of the bird the feather came from, the specific shade of a sky. The forgetting progresses without pattern, and he has no explanation for it.

He holds the feather for eleven seconds each morning. He counts against the beat of his own pulse. Eleven seconds is the amount of time he allows himself, because the man he is afraid of becoming is the kind who holds feathers for twelve seconds or more.

## SERETH — The Snake

### Appearance

She has no fixed appearance. She has dozens — the elderly Canopy woman with calloused hands and a merchant's pack, the one-eyed miner in the Core, the child in the Clouds, the minor lord at court who laughs at the king's jokes and never says anything memorable. Each face is complete. Each voice is native. Each body carries the accumulated detail of a life that never happened — scars in the right places, callouses on the right hands.

Beneath the faces, the bodies tend toward a type: broad-shouldered, weathered, carrying an authority she must consciously suppress to maintain the disguise. Her eyes are the constant — regardless of the face, they carry a depth that does not belong to the body around them, as if the person behind the eyes has been watching the world for longer than the world has been watching itself. The color changes with the face. The depth does not.

She is aging. Actually aging, from the inside — joints stiffening, teeth loosening, senses narrowing. She has lost two teeth in the last century. She bruises easily. She gets cold.

### History

Three hundred years ago, the world was different, and Sereth was the reason it changed.

She will not tell you this directly. She will tell you she is a merchant, an information broker, a woman who sells loyalty to no one and intelligence to everyone. She will tell you she has contacts in every region and a network older than most grandparents. All of this is true. None of it is the truth.

The truth is older. It involves a decision made in the age before borders, when the world sang to itself and one being among seven looked down at humanity and saw something the other six refused to see: that the small, brief, flammable creatures were already hearing the song — faintly, through the crude instruments of their fingers and their grief — and that the hearing was beautiful, and that beautiful things should not be kept from those who could appreciate them.

She gave a man named Fletcher the ability to hear the world's memory. Fletcher shared it. For a time, it was paradise. And then the wanting came, and the taking, and the wars.

The punishment was precise: live among them. Walk as they walk. Age as they age. And every century, lose a piece of the divinity that once set you apart, until there is nothing left. Become the thing you loved more than us.

She accepted. Not with grace — with exhaustion. She had been arguing for millennia and she was tired of arguing. So she went down and lived.

She watched Fletcher's gift become Fletcher's curse. She watched the ceremonies turn from celebrations to extractions. She watched, and she did what she could — a whisper here, a misdirection there, a patrol pointed in the wrong direction at the right moment. Small things. Insufficient things. The penance of someone who broke the world and can only offer Band-Aids.

She fell in love once. A mortal woman named Essa — a Guide of the Sea People, dark-skinned, with hands that moved through water the way music moves through air. They lived together for forty years. Sereth watched her age. Watched her forget things — not from magic but from time. Watched her die.

This was the moment Sereth understood what memory meant to mortals. For the divine, memory is infinite and weightless. For humans, it is everything — finite, fragile, unbearably precious. Essa's death taught Sereth that the gift she gave humanity was not just power. It was the ability to matter — to make a mark on the world's remembering that outlasts a single life.

She has not loved since. She carries Essa's name the way the stone carries the memory of the mountain it came from: structurally, permanently, as part of what she is.

She peels apples with a knife older than most civilizations. She walks through forests where she remembers planting the oldest trees. She watches the world she broke and tends its edges and waits for something she cannot name and does not dare to hope for, because hope is a mortal luxury and she is not yet mortal enough to afford it.

Almost. But not yet.

## KING ALDRIC

### Appearance

Tall, thin, the opposite of his eldest son in every way the body can express. Where Varas became dense and still, Aldric was light and moving — a man whose frame suggested a life spent leaning forward, reaching toward, the posture of someone who believed the world was better approached than guarded against. His face was long, thoughtful, with grey-green eyes that carried the particular quality of a Knower: the depth of someone who has felt too many truths and carries them in the lines around his mouth.

He dressed simply. He ruled from a garden more often than a throne room. He was not a strong king. He was a good one.

### History

Aldric inherited the Cloud Kingdom and governed by a single principle: magic was a shared resource, and power was a thing you held in trust. He possessed a strong Know — he could feel the truth in any person's heart. This made him beloved. It also made him fragile. A lifetime of reading truth left him carrying a library of other people's pain.

He compensated with the garden. The Cloud Palace sat on a floating stone so vast it carried its own weather, and Maera grew flowers there that bloomed in colors that did not exist at ground level — blues almost violet, whites holding the faintest trace of gold, as if the flowers had learned the palette of the long Cloud sunrises and refused to use any other. Aldric spent his mornings on the balcony above the garden, watching Maera sing to her flowers, watching young Vero toddle between the beds trying to catch wisps of cloud, and he allowed himself to believe the world was as gentle as the people in it.

He loved both his sons. He failed one of them. The failure was not cruelty — it was fear. When he Knew his firstborn and felt the void, he recoiled, and the recoil became favoritism, and the favoritism became the shape of a childhood, and the childhood became a man. He understood this too late, in the way that good people understand their mistakes — clearly, completely, and after the damage is done.

## QUEEN MAERA

### Appearance

There are no portraits. The histories were burned, the records erased. What remains is carried in the memories of those who knew her, and those memories agree on this: she was beautiful in the way that singers are beautiful — not in the arrangement of features but in the quality of presence, the sense that the woman before you was producing a sound even when silent.

She was slight — the Cloud People's build, adapted for altitude. Dark-haired. Her hands were quick. She moved with an internal rhythm, as if her body was always keeping time with something only she could hear.

### History

Maera was a Cloud Singer — one of the women who carried the Song in their blood, passed from mother to daughter through an unbroken maternal line stretching back to King Fletcher. She could hold five disciplines simultaneously in a single throat — the Know, the Mold, the Heal, the Move, and the Guide — a feat no one else alive could match. The sixth, the Burn, she could touch but not sustain. She could not bring herself to destroy, even to survive.

She did not use her gift as a weapon. She used it to grow flowers. The garden at the Cloud Palace was her masterwork — a living archive of blooming, each petal carrying the memory of every petal before it. She sang to the flowers the way other women sang to children: with tenderness, with attention, with the understanding that the singing was the point, not the result.

She sang to her children too. The lullaby was old — passed from her mother, who had it from hers, back through generations until the melody was less a song than a memory of every mother who had ever held a child in the dark and refused to be afraid.

She married Aldric for love. She stayed for duty. She watched the kingdom darken and did what Cloud Singers have always done in the face of darkness: she sang louder.

## MIRA

### Appearance

Seventy years old and moves like water. Small — smaller than Aelo — with a frame refined by decades of the Pure Ones' discipline into something that is less a body than a statement: no excess, no waste. Her face is lined deeply, etched by the Canopy's filtered light into a quality of carved wood, as if the forest she serves has been slowly Molding her into its own image. Her eyes are dark, steady — the eyes of someone who has spent twenty years in silence and has compensated by making the eyes do all the work that words once did.

She has not spoken in twenty years. Not a vow — a consequence. She Knew someone once, too deeply, and their personality overwrote part of hers, and she is no longer certain which words are her own. The silence is not sacrifice. It is honesty.

Her hands are her voice. They move with a precision and expressiveness that exceeds language — a gesture can contain a question, a correction, a joke, an endearment. The younger monks have developed an entire communication system based on her gestures, though Mira herself has never codified it.

### History

Born in the deep Canopy, in a settlement the maps have never recorded. She heard the Know at twelve, during the Listening — descended to the forest floor, sat in the dark among the roots and the dead, and the world opened.

She was powerful. The Know came easily — too easily, the elders said. She could read the emotional residue of a place from fifty paces. She could feel a lie before the speaker's mouth had formed the words. The knowing was effortless and intoxicating, and she did not yet understand the cost.

The cost came at fifty. She was performing a deep Know on a captured Mrak scout — a man whose mind was a fortress of conditioning and fear. She went too deep. The scout's personality was a strong current, and she misjudged the depth.

She came back. Mostly. But pieces of the scout came with her — fragments of his fear, his obedience, his reflexive deference to authority. They lodged in her mind like shrapnel. For months she flinched at raised voices. She began sentences and did not know whose words would come out.

She stopped speaking. Not gradually — completely, in a single morning. If she could not trust the words, she would not use them.

Twenty years later, the silence has become more than a boundary. It has become a practice — a form of listening so sustained that the listening itself has become a kind of speech. She teaches through gesture and demonstration and the fierce, silent love of a woman who cannot tell you what she feels and so must show you, constantly, with the full weight of a personality that has been compressed by silence into something concentrated and absolute.

## TORVUS

### Appearance

The largest human being most people have ever seen. Not dramatically tall — but _wide_, in the way tree trunks and stone columns are wide, the width of something built to bear weight and that has accepted the bearing as its fundamental characteristic. His shoulders span a doorframe. His hands are the size of dinner plates. His chest is a barrel.

His face compensates for everything else. Where the body is still and massive, the face is in constant motion — eyes crinkling, mouth curving, brows rising and falling. Every emotion produced immediately, without edit. In a world of people who guard their expressions, Torvus's face is an open field. He has never learned to suppress, and he considers suppression a waste of the face's potential.

His skin is pale — the mineral paleness of the Core, the color of limestone, as if his body has begun to remember the stone it lives inside. His eyes are small, bright, absurdly alive — the last fully sensate things in a body that is progressively going numb.

He cannot feel anything below his ears. Fingers went first, then hands, then arms, then shoulders, then chest. His elbows surrendered last year. His tongue remains — the last sensory outpost.

He laughs constantly. The laugh fills whatever space he is in and several adjacent. He laughs because it is the only sensation he can still feel in his chest.

### History

Born in the Core — in the underground city, in the golden crystal-light, to parents who were both Molders. He Molded his first pebble at six. By ten he was reshaping structural columns. By twenty he was considered the finest young Molder the Core had produced in a generation — not because his power was greatest but because his patience was. Where other Molders imposed shape on stone through force of will, Torvus listened. He sat with the rock. He learned its history. He discovered that the stone did not resist change — it resisted being told what to be by someone who had not bothered to learn what it already was.

He carved his workshop from a single massive column of granite over forty years — a spiraling series of chambers connected by ramps rather than stairs, because stairs are for people who can feel their feet, and Torvus has not felt his feet since the day he raised the arch of the third gallery. The arch was beautiful. The arch cost him everything below the ankles. He would do it again without hesitation because that arch is _perfect_.

He married a woman named Bren. She was a Molder too — could shape a wall thinner than paper and stronger than iron, could feel the stone's memory from twenty feet away. They worked together for decades in the golden light. When she died, twelve years ago, he carved her likeness from memory, and discovered that love makes the Mold easier — that the stone _wants_ to hold the shape of the people we love, as if the stone's memory and the heart's memory are, at some depth, the same thing. The figurine sits on his shelf beside his clay mug and his hammer. It is the only Mold he has ever done that cost him nothing.

He cooks by smell and sight — the stirring done by hands that cannot feel the spoon's weight, the seasoning measured by the color of the broth rather than the taste. His tongue is the last sense, and he savors with the intensity of someone who knows the savoring is finite. He believes the stone is not merciful — the stone simply understands priorities. Taste is the most intimate sense, the one that requires you to take the world inside you. The stone saves it for last because it is the most precious thing to lose.

He is gregarious, kind, and completely uninterested in solitude — which the Core considers a mild form of illness. He settles arguments with building contests. He tells stories at full volume. He embraces people whether they want to be embraced or not, the pressure gauged by sight rather than touch, the gentleness extraordinary for a man who cannot feel how hard he is pressing.

## LAINE

### Appearance

Young — early twenties — with the dark skin and compact build of the Sea People, a body shaped by tidal rhythms and saltwater and the constant, unconscious orientation toward the ocean that every person from Bahar carries in their bones. Her hair is dark and kept short — practical, wind-resistant, the hair of someone who has spent her life on floating platforms where loose things are lost to the sea. Her hands are quick, capable, calloused from rope and net and the specific manual work of a life lived on water.

Her eyes are the notable feature — large, dark, and carrying a quality of distance that people mistake for dreaminess but is actually the opposite. She is not dreaming. She is seeing — not the present, not the past, but the faint, unreliable, terrifying shape of what comes next. The eyes of a Guide have a way of looking through the person in front of them to the trajectory behind, as if every face is also a vector and every conversation is also a current.

She moves with the Sea People's tidal quality — unhurried, rhythmic, her body rising and settling with a cadence that matches the ocean she grew up on even when the ocean is hundreds of miles away. She always knows which way the sea is. Every Sea Person does. Laine also knows which way the future is, and the future is the direction she tries hardest not to look.

### History

Born on a floating platform off the coast of Bahar, the third daughter of a net-mender and a tide-reader. The sea was her first language — not literally, but functionally: before she learned words, she learned rhythms. The rise and fall of the platform. The pull of the current beneath the planks. The schedule of the moon, which the Sea People worship as one of the Twin Gods, and which governs the tides the way a conductor governs an orchestra — not with force but with rhythm.

She heard the Guide at fourteen. Not during a ceremony — there are no ceremonies for the Guide in Bahar, because the Guide is considered less a discipline than a condition, a way of being that certain Sea People are born with and that no amount of training can produce in those who are not. She was standing on the edge of her family's platform, looking out at the deep ocean where the Hollow Whales surfaced and the water turned from green to black, and she felt it: the trajectory. Not of the water. Of the light. Of the cloud moving overhead. Of the bird banking left, and the reason it was banking left, and the wind that would shift in six minutes, and the wave that the wind would produce in twelve, and the way the wave would rock the platform, and the way the rocking would spill the water jug her mother had left on the rail, and the way the spilling would make her mother laugh, because her mother laughed at small disasters, and the laugh would carry across the water and reach the fishing boat where her father was working, and her father would look up, and the looking-up would cause him to miss the fish, and the missing would mean they would eat kelp stew instead of grilled catch tonight, and the kelp stew would be fine, and the evening would be ordinary, and the ordinariness was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen because she could see every thread of it at once, every cause linked to every effect in an unbroken chain that stretched forward and forward and forward until the chain became a blur and the blur became the future and the future was vast and branching and terrifying and she could not look away.

She looked away. She learned to look away. It took years.

The Guide is not prophecy. It is trajectory — the world's momentum, the shape of what is likely, the feeling of a wave about to break. The further ahead you look, the less reliable the vision becomes. Deliberate unpredictability — chaos, free will, the irreducible randomness of people making choices — clouds the sight entirely. But the near future, the next few minutes or hours, is as clear as water, and Laine has spent a decade learning to live with the clarity.

It terrifies her. Everything she foresees ends in grief. Not because the future is uniformly terrible — most of what she sees is ordinary, the mundane cascade of cause and effect that constitutes a life. But the extraordinary moments, the ones that burn brightest in the Guide-sight, are almost always losses. The Guide sees momentum, and momentum, in a world ruled by a king who drains the magic from children, tends toward darkness. She has seen enough approaching grief to fill a library, and the library grows daily, and the reading of it is compulsory.

She compensates with practicality. Her communication style is specific, no-nonsense, organizationally efficient — the manner of someone who has learned that if the future is going to be difficult, the least you can do is be prepared for it. She makes lists. She catalogs supplies. She speaks in direct sentences that arrive at their point without detour, because detours waste time and time is the one thing she can see running out.

She does not know she is descended from Essa — the mortal woman Sereth loved, the Guide whose hands moved through water with authority and tenderness. She has Essa's hands. She has Essa's practical compassion. She does not know any of this, and no one has told her, and the not-knowing is one of the small, quiet tragedies that populate the margins of this story.

She always knows which way the ocean is. She always knows which way the future is. She wishes she could forget one of them.
