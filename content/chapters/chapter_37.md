# Chapter 37

## The Throne Room

---

The throne room was the silence made architectural.

Aelo entered through a corridor that had straightened as he walked — the Song's resonance peeling back decades of Varas's organic Molding, the stone remembering Fletcher's original design, the clean lines and honest angles of a room that had been built for governance and had been converted into a feeding apparatus. The corridor opened onto the great hall. The hall was vast; not spatially but existentially. The vastness of absence.

The walls were covered in Blood Vine — the roots thicker here than anywhere else in the castle, the arterial network converging on a single point the way rivers converge on a delta. The Vine ran in organized channels from every surface to the center of the room, where the channels met and merged and descended into the floor and from the floor into the Chamber below, the circulatory system connecting the king on the throne to the prisoners in the dark.

The Elder Stones were everywhere. Dozens of them. Embedded in the walls, in the floor, in the throne itself — each one connected by Vine to the network, each one pulsing with the sickly amber luminescence of stolen magic. The Stones were not singing. The Stones were _humming_: the low, operational frequency of a system in use, the sound of extraction, the sound of memory being pulled from flesh and channeled through crystal and delivered to the man who sat at the center of the web.

Varas.

He was on the obsidian throne. He was enormous — not in height but in mass, in _density_, the body expanded by decades of consumption into something that was barely human. His skin had the texture of bark, the Blood Vine's growths having spread from his hands up his arms and across his torso and onto his face, the organic material merging with the flesh, the boundary between man and parasite dissolved by forty years of sustained, intimate, continuous contact. His hands, the bark-textured hands that rested on the armrests, were more Vine than flesh, the fingers thick, dark, the joints swollen with the accumulated growths. His eyes, which had been the color of Elder Stone for decades, were fading. The Song's resonance in the walls was weakening the Vine's connection, and the amber was draining from his irises the way color drains from a flame when the fuel runs low. What remained was grey. Flat. Carrying no more emotional content than the obsidian beneath him.

He was connected to everything. The Vine ran from his body into the throne, from the throne into the floor, from the floor into the walls and the Stones and the network that extended through the castle and into the Murkr and into the borders of the dead zone where the tendrils thinned and vanished into the earth. He was the castle. The castle was him. The distinction had ceased to exist decades ago — the man and the building merged into a single organism, the parasitic architecture serving the parasitic king, the draining and the being-drained indistinguishable.

And he was smiling.

The smile was wrong. Not because it was cruel or mocking or villainous — because it was _genuine_. The bark-textured face, which Aelo's dimmed Know could barely read through the Murkr's dead zone, was producing an expression that was not performance but response. The smile of a man who has been waiting and the waiting is over.

"Finally," Varas said. His voice was soft. Not the softness of a whisper but the softness of a voice that had no reason to be loud because the room was empty and the emptiness was the only audience the voice had ever had. "I've been waiting for someone who could make the stone sing."

Aelo stood in the doorway. The Song hummed in his blood — the full chord, all seven voices, sustained by Maera's lullaby and Jalo's last gift and the accumulated resonance of every discipline and every cost and every act of listening that had brought him to this room. The humming was not deliberate. It was involuntary, the blood responding to the proximity of the Elder Stones and the Vine and the convergence point where the world's memory had been strongest and where the draining had made it weakest.

Varas could feel it. Aelo could see the feeling on the bark-textured face — the slight widening of the stone-colored eyes, the almost imperceptible tilt of the head, the body language of a man encountering a stimulus that his senses were not equipped to fully process but that produced, nonetheless, a response.

"You're Fletcher's blood. I can feel it." The soft voice carried across the empty hall. The Blood Vine in the walls pulsed — the network responding to the king's attention, the organism orienting toward the stimulus. "You're humming — did you know that? Your blood is humming. It's the most beautiful sound I've ever almost heard."

"Almost?"

"I can't hear it. Not properly. I've never been able to hear it." Varas leaned forward on the throne. The movement was slow, the body's mass requiring effort, the bark-textured limbs heavy with decades of growth. "That was always the cruelty, wasn't it? Born into a world of music and deaf from the first breath."

---

Aelo walked into the hall. Not toward the throne — toward the center. The place where the corridors converged, where the Blood Vine's channels met, where the floor vibrated with the accumulated traffic of decades of extracted memory flowing from the Chamber to the throne. He stood in the center and he looked at the king and the king looked back and the looking was not the assessment of enemies but the attendance of two people who were, for the first time, seeing each other clearly.

"You killed people," Aelo said. "Thousands. You drained them. You put them in the Chamber and you connected them to your Stones and you consumed their memories until there was nothing left."

"Yes."

"You killed my mother."

"I ordered the nursery cleared. I did not know she would Sing. No one had Sung in generations. When the soldiers reported that a woman was holding a note that they could not pass — that their own blood was refusing to obey them — I was... interested." The stone eyes held a distance that was not emotional but structural. "I have spent my life being interested in the things I cannot feel. Your mother's Song was the most interesting thing that ever happened in my presence. I ordered them to wait. To let her Sing. I wanted to hear it."

"Did you?"

"Almost. The way I almost hear your blood. A vibration. A pressure against the barrier. The shape of a sound without the sound itself." He paused. The pause was not the Ming's deliberate interval or Jalo's defensive silence. It was the pause of a man who was describing the central condition of his existence and who was, for the first time, describing it to someone who might be capable of understanding. "She held the note for seven minutes. I listened for seven minutes. And for seven minutes, the barrier was thinner than it had ever been — thin enough that I could almost, _almost_ feel what was on the other side. And then the Molders broke through the wall and the note ended and the barrier returned and the almost was gone."

"And you've been trying to get it back ever since."

"I have been trying to get it back since I was born. The note was not the beginning. The note was the most recent failure in a lifetime of failures. Every person I drained, every Stone I fed from, every life I consumed — each one was an attempt. Each one gave me half a second. Half a second of almost-feeling, of the barrier thinning, of the ghost of an emotion pressing against the walls of a room that has no door. And then the half-second ended and I needed more."

He looked at Aelo. The stone eyes — flat, carrying no emotional content that the Know could read because there was no emotional content to read — held the boy's face with an attention that was not the attention of a predator or a king but the attention of a patient describing symptoms to the first doctor who has asked.

"I didn't want to be a monster," Varas said. The words were flat. Not performed. Stated. The way a man with a terminal diagnosis states the facts of his condition. "I wanted to hear the music. I took because I was never given. I drained because I could not fill. Every life I consumed, I got half a second of what you feel constantly — the world, speaking. And then it faded, and I needed more. And the more was never enough. And the never-enough was the hunger. And the hunger was the only thing I could feel."

"Then stop."

"I can't." The word was not defiance. It was diagnosis. "Stopping is silence. And silence is all I've ever known. You don't understand what it's like to live in a world that sings and hear nothing. You, with your gifts, your sensitivities, your tears over dead birds —" The bark-textured face produced something that was not quite a smile and not quite a grimace. "I know about the bird. The Guardian's memories — some of them reached me through the network. A boy, crying in a yard, holding a small dead thing. The Guardian's love for the boy was so strong that even the draining could not strip it completely. I consumed his memories for fifteen years and in every one of them, the love was the loudest thing."

The words landed in the empty hall with a weight that Aelo had not expected — the weight of a man who had consumed Jalo's memories and had found, in every one, the same thing: the love. The love that the draining could not strip. The love that had been the loudest thing in the room, louder than the extraction, louder than the silence.

"You felt it," Aelo said. "You felt his love."

"Almost. The way I almost feel everything. Enough to know it was there. Not enough to know what it was."

Aelo looked at the king. And for the first time, he did what his magic was born to do. He did not read Varas's emotions — there were none to read. He did not enter Varas's memories — the memories were stolen, not his. He _felt_ Varas. The void. The hollow where a soul should be. The structural, architectural absence that was not the result of damage or choice but of design — the building built without a door, the room without an exit, the life lived inside a silence so total that the silence had become the identity and the identity had consumed the world in a futile, desperate, seventy-year attempt to hear the one thing the identity could not produce.

Varas was not evil. Varas was in pain. A pain so absolute and so primal — the pain of being excluded from existence itself — that it had warped him into the thing he was. The Chamber. The draining. The Dog Scene. The purge. The Kingdom of nothing — all of it was not cruelty but _reaching_. The four-year-old boy in Aldric's memory, hands up, wanting to be held. Reaching. Wanting. Unable to close the gap between himself and the world that everyone else inhabited and that he had been locked out of since birth.

"I'm sorry," Aelo said.

Varas blinked. The stone eyes — which had not changed expression since Aelo entered the room — changed. Not dramatically. A fractional adjustment. The muscles around the eyes contracting by a millimeter. The involuntary, uncontrollable response of a person who has been spoken to with a word they have never heard directed at them before.

In seventy years of rule, through executions and drainings and the feast and the purge and the Chamber — no one had ever said that to him.

"Don't," Varas said. His voice cracked. Barely. A hairline fracture in the flat, soft, empty voice. "Don't you dare."

"I'm going to Sing."

"I know."

"It will undo everything you've built."

"I know."

"The Song will reach the Chamber. It will reach the prisoners. It will reach the Stones and the Vine and the network. It will remind the world that the memory is not yours — that the memory belongs to the people you took it from. The draining will reverse. The stolen memories will return. The Chamber will empty. The Murkr will heal. And you —" Aelo paused. He looked at the man on the throne. The man who had consumed the world. The man who had never heard the Song. "You will lose everything."

"Everything I have is stolen. Losing it is not a loss. It's an accounting."

"You could stop me."

"I could." Varas was quiet. The Blood Vine pulsed — the network awaiting instruction, the organism ready to act, the castle's immune system poised to attack the boy who was standing in its center and who was carrying in his blood the one thing the system could not tolerate. The king could give the order. The Vine would come. The extraction would begin. The Song in the boy's blood would be drained and consumed and the draining would give Varas more than half a second — would give him, perhaps, a full second of almost-hearing, a full second of the barrier thinning, a full second of the ghost pressing against the walls.

The Vine moved.

Aelo felt it before he saw it — a tendril detaching from the wall behind the throne, thick, dark, the oldest growth, curling toward him with the slow, deliberate momentum of something that had been feeding for forty years and that recognized, in the boy's blood, the richest meal it had ever been offered. A second tendril followed. A third. The network responding not to a command but to appetite — the system's autonomous reflex, the organism doing what the organism had been designed to do, the way a heart beats without being told.

Varas's hands tightened on the armrests. The bark-textured fingers dug into the obsidian. The tendrils advanced — three feet from Aelo, two feet, the tips reaching, the Blood Vine's draining mechanism already activating, the cold pull beginning to tug at Aelo's diminished Know the way gravity tugs at a body on a ledge.

Aelo did not move. He held the lake stone. He held the riverbed. He held the man's gaze — the stone eyes that were open and warring, the muscles beneath the bark-texture clenching and releasing, the face of a man whose body was doing one thing and whose mind was trying to do another and the distance between the two was seventy years of habit fighting one moment of choice.

"Stop," Varas said. Not to Aelo. To the Vine. To himself. The word came out fractured — half command, half plea, the voice of a man who had never had to override his own appetite because the appetite had never been contradicted. The tendrils slowed. They did not stop. The Vine did not understand stopping. The Vine understood feeding. The Vine had been fed for four decades and the feeding was not a behavior but an identity, and identities do not respond to single words spoken by the men who built them.

Varas's hands lifted from the armrests. The lifting was physical effort — visible, straining, the bark-textured fingers peeling away from the obsidian with the reluctance of a thing that had grown into its seat and that was being asked to ungrow. He pressed his palms together. The gesture was not prayer. The gesture was containment — the hands holding each other because the hands, left free, would give the order the mouth was refusing to give. The tendrils trembled. They hung in the air between Varas and Aelo — two feet from the boy's face, the tips pulsing, the draining mechanism cycling without a target, the appetite circling.

The war lasted nine seconds. Aelo counted them in heartbeats. Nine seconds during which the most powerful man in the world fought the only enemy he had never conquered: himself. The void wanted. The void had always wanted. The void was the wanting, and the wanting was reaching for the one thing that might fill it, and the man who housed the void was holding his own hands together and saying _stop_ to a body that had never been told _no_ by the mind that lived inside it.

On the tenth second, the tendrils withdrew. Slowly — pulling back into the wall, the tips retracting, the appetite unsatisfied but overruled. The Blood Vine settled. The network stood down. The air in the throne room, which had been compressed by the Vine's activation, expanded — a breath the room had been holding, released.

Varas's hands separated. They returned to the armrests. They were shaking. The bark-textured fingers, which had been steady for forty years because steadiness was the byproduct of a man who never hesitated, were trembling with the effort of the first hesitation.

"But then I'd never hear it," Varas said.

The words sat in the empty hall. The Blood Vine pulsed. The Elder Stones hummed. The silence held — the charged, unbreakable silence of a man who had spent his life consuming the world and who was now, in the final moment, choosing to be consumed instead. Not because the sorry had reached him or the boy's compassion had pierced the void.

Because the void was so vast and so cold that even the man who had caused all of this would rather hear one note of the Song and die than live another day in silence.

"Sing," Varas said. "Let me hear it. One note. One real note. Not the ghost. Not the almost. The thing itself. Let me hear what the world sounds like to everyone except me."

He closed his stone eyes. The Blood Vine stilled. The network standing down, the immune system disengaging, the castle ceasing its defense. The king surrendered. To the need. The first need. The only need. The need that had been the engine of every horror and every atrocity and every consumed life: the need to hear.

Then the stone eyes opened again. And the thing behind them was not the king. Not the consumer. Not the man who had built a kingdom of silence to fill a silence that could not be filled. The thing behind the eyes was older than all of that. Smaller. Younger. Still reaching.

"Before you go." The voice cracked further. The hairline fracture widening. "What does it sound like? Music. I have consumed ten thousand memories of music and I have the data: the pitch, the frequency, the duration, the mathematical structure. I have all of it. I have none of it." The bark-textured hands gripped each other in his lap. "What does it _feel_ like? To hear a song. Any song. Not the Song. A song. The kind that — that mothers —"

He stopped. The word _mothers_ sat in the room like a stone dropped into still water.

Aelo looked at the man on the throne. The man who had never been sung to. The man whose father had recognized the void too late and whose mother had died when the void was already set and whose childhood had been a series of rooms in which people spoke and laughed and sang and the speaking and the laughing and the singing arrived as data. Pressure waves, measurable, documentable. Empty of the thing that made them music.

Aelo did not answer the question.

He hummed.

Quiet. Barely audible. Maera's lullaby. The seventh note, the contribution his mother had held as the door broke down and the fire came and the shield went up. The note she had sung to him in the nursery. The note Jalo had carried in his memory for fifteen years. The note that had survived every draining because the love in it was louder than the extraction.

The sound was small. A boy humming in a dark room. The acoustics were wrong; the throne room was built for authority, for the amplification of commands, and the lullaby was built for the opposite, for the narrowing of the world to a single voice and a single ear, for the space between a mother and a child.

Varas's hands stopped gripping each other.

His head tilted. A fractional movement, the involuntary orientation of a body toward a sound it is straining to hear, the way a plant orients toward light it cannot quite reach. The stone eyes were open and the stone eyes were wet. Not tears. The bark-textured face could not produce tears. Moisture. The body's mechanical response to something the body did not have the architecture to process. The barrier was there. The barrier was always there. But the lullaby pressed against it, gently, the way a hand presses against a window, and for a fraction of a second, less than the half-second at the Elder Stone, a fraction of a fraction, the barrier thinned.

He did not hear the lullaby. He heard the ghost of the ghost of it. An echo of an echo. The faintest possible evidence that on the other side of the barrier, in the world he had been locked out of since birth, a sound existed. It was kind. The kindness was meant for him.

His mouth opened. Closed. Opened again. The bark-textured lips trying to form a word and failing because the word required a category of feeling that the void did not contain and that the ghost of the ghost had, for one fraction, suggested the existence of.

Aelo stopped humming. The lullaby faded. The silence returned, but the silence was different now. Thinner. As if the lullaby had worn a small, permanent groove in it, a thread-thin channel through which the faintest whisper of sound could, if the world was very quiet and the listening was very deep, pass.

"That," Varas whispered. "That was —"

"My mother's."

The two words landed in the throne room with a weight that had nothing to do with volume. Varas had consumed Maera's memories through the network. He had drained the woman's son and the woman's guardian and the woman's lullaby had been in the data somewhere, filed, cataloged, stripped of its warmth. And now the woman's son was standing in front of him and had hummed it. Freely. For the man who had destroyed everything the woman had loved.

Varas's hands ungripped. They lay open in his lap, palms up, the bark-textured fingers uncurled, the posture of a man receiving something he cannot hold. The posture of a four-year-old boy, hands up, waiting.

"The Song will be louder," Aelo said. "The Song will reach you. I promise."

He turned. He walked toward the corridor that led down. Down to the Chamber, down to the prisoners, down to the place where Sereth was waiting and the Blood Vine was ready and the Song was about to be Sung.

Behind him, Varas sat on his throne with his hands open in his lap and the ghost of a ghost in his ears. And the waiting was the first patient thing the man had done in seventy years. And the patience was not the void's appetite dressed in stillness. The patience was the lullaby's residue: the thread-thin groove it had worn in the silence, the evidence that sound existed, the promise that more was coming.

He waited the way a child waits for a voice in the dark. He had been waiting his entire life. He could wait a little longer.
