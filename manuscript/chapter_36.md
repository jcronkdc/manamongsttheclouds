# Chapter 36

## The Arrival

---

Sereth entered the Murkr in their true form, and the Murkr tried to erase them.

Not the fisherman. Not the old woman. Not any of the borrowed faces that three centuries of mortality had taught them to wear. Themselves. The original shape — or what remained of it, which was less than it had been a decade ago and less than that the decade before, the divinity leaking out of them in a slow, steady, irreversible diminishment that had been their condition since the other six had stripped them down and sent them to walk among the creatures they had loved too much. The true form was not dramatic. Not the towering, luminous, world-shaping presence that the stories described — the stories were wrong, the stories were always wrong, the stories assumed that gods looked like their power and Sereth had learned, over three centuries, that gods looked like their choices, and Sereth's choices had made them look like a person who was very tired and very old and very nearly finished.

They were small. Smaller than the fisherman, smaller than the old woman. The body that remained when all the borrowed shapes were set aside was compact, worn, the surface weathered by the specific erosion of divinity leaving flesh — the skin thinner than a mortal's, the bones lighter, the muscles reduced to the minimum required for locomotion. One eye. The left was gone — the original wound, the price of the original gift, the cost of giving humanity the Song. The remaining eye was not brown or black or any human color. It was the color of the world's first light — a gold so deep it was almost amber, carrying in its iris the compressed visual memory of a being that had seen the world's beginning and was now, in all probability, seeing the approach of its own ending.

The Murkr hit them like a wall.

The dead zone was designed — by Varas, unknowingly, through decades of draining — to repel divine memory. The oldest memory in the world. The memory that predated the disciplines, predated Fletcher, predated the Song. Sereth's memory was the oldest thing walking, and the Murkr's anti-memory field targeted it with the specific, automated hostility of a system encountering the thing it was built to destroy. Walking through it was like walking upstream in a river of forgetting. Each step cost them something.

The color of the sky the day they gave Fletcher the gift — gone. A pale blue, lighter than the Cloud People's formations, carrying the specific luminosity of a world that was about to change forever. Gone. The step took it and the taking was not violent but patient, the Murkr absorbing the memory the way sand absorbs water, the divine color sinking into the grey and vanishing.

The sound of the other gods' voices — gone. Six voices, each one carrying the frequency of an element, the collective chorus that had been the universe's first council and that had debated, for longer than humanity had existed, the question of whether the creatures below deserved the gift. Gone. The step took it and the silence where the voices had been was the specific silence of a room that had been full and was now empty and that would never be full again.

The feeling of being infinite — gone. Not the infinity of power but the infinity of perspective, the specific, divine quality of seeing everything at once, every moment simultaneous, every life a note in a chord that was the universe. Gone. The step took it and what remained was the mortal's perspective: linear, limited, moving through time rather than containing it.

They did not care. This was the truth that the stories would never capture: Sereth walked into the Murkr and lost the last pieces of their divinity and did not care, because the caring had been the first thing the mortality had given them and the caring was worth more than the infinity had ever been. The infinity had been beautiful. The caring was _necessary_. The infinity had allowed them to see everything. The caring had taught them to see the boy.

They walked. The grey pressed. The divine memory fell away. And Sereth became, with each step, more mortal and less god and closer to the thing they had been trying to be for three hundred years: a person. Simply a person. A person who had made a choice and was walking toward the consequence of that choice and was willing to arrive at the consequence with nothing left except the choice itself.

---

They found Laine at the border.

She was where Jalo's Mold command had carried her — at the edge of the Murkr, the grey beginning to thin, the living world visible beyond as a line of muted color. She was unconscious. The Vine-burns on her legs were dark — the magical residue leaving marks that were not physical injuries but memory injuries, the flesh damaged not by heat but by extraction, the skin carrying the specific, visible evidence of a place where the Blood Vine had touched and taken.

Sereth knelt beside her. They looked at her hands. Small, angular, the nails short, the fingers callused from years of coastal work and stone-throwing and the specific, physical labor of a woman who had lived on the Sea and had used her hands for everything because the Guide was not a physical discipline and the body needed to do something while the mind read trajectories.

Essa's hands. The same shape. The same proportions. Three hundred years and the hands had survived — not the specific hands, not the cells or the skin or the particular arrangement of bone and tendon, but the _shape_. The genetic memory of Essa's hands, carried through generations of daughters, arriving here, in this woman, on this border, at this moment.

Sereth allowed themselves one moment of grief. One moment. The discipline of three centuries — the practice of losing, of letting go, of watching the things they loved die and be born and die again — permitted one moment. They looked at Essa's hands on Laine's wrists and they remembered: Essa's hands braiding rope on a dock in Bahar. Essa's hands cupping water from a stream. Essa's hands on Sereth's face, the night Essa understood what Sereth was and chose to love them anyway. Essa's hands, old and spotted, the last time Sereth held them, the night Essa died.

One moment. Then the moment ended and the grief was filed and the work began.

They Healed her. The last significant act of magic they might ever perform — the last flicker of the divine power that had been leaking out of them for three centuries and that was, in the Murkr's anti-memory field, guttering to its final spark. They placed their hands on Laine's legs — the Vine-burns dark and spreading — and they _remembered_. Not the Heal discipline — the divine version. The original act of mending that had existed before the disciplines were divided, before Fletcher, before the Song. The direct, unmediated, god-to-flesh restoration of a body's memory of wholeness, performed not through a discipline but through the specific, fading, almost-extinguished authority of a being that had been present when the body was first designed and that could still, barely, remind the flesh of its original specifications.

The burns healed. The dark marks faded. The memory-damage repaired — not fully, not completely, but enough. Enough to restore consciousness. Enough to give the Guide-sight a chance of reassembling itself from the shattered pieces that the Vine's contact had scattered.

The healing cost Sereth. They felt it leave — the last significant quantity of divinity, the last measurable reserve, departing through their hands and into Laine's flesh and disappearing, the divine power converting to human healing and being consumed by the conversion. What remained after was not a god. Not even the remnant of a god. A person. A very old, very tired, nearly mortal person with one eye and a body that was more fragile than it had been five minutes ago and a purpose that was clearer than it had been in three centuries.

Laine's eyes opened. The restless eyes — the Guide eyes, dark and trajectory-reading — were different. The restlessness was gone. The eyes were still. Not the focused stillness of concentration or the blank stillness of damage. A new stillness. The stillness of eyes that had been shattered and were reassembling and that were, in the reassembling, seeing the world with a clarity that the restlessness had never permitted.

She saw Sereth. The true face — not old, not young, not human, not god. Something in between. Something that had been in between for a very long time and that was now, in the aftermath of the healing, tipping decisively toward the human side, the divine features softening, the gold eye dimming toward amber, the face becoming the face of a person who had once been something else and who was now, irrevocably, this.

"Who are you?"

"Someone who has been watching over your family for three hundred years." Sereth's voice was different in the true form — not the fisherman's rolling baritone or the old woman's Canopy-accented contralto. Thinner. Quieter. The voice of a being that had been speaking for longer than language had existed and that was, in these final hours, running out of things to say. "And I need your help."

Laine's Guide-sight — damaged, reassembling, operating at a fraction of its former capacity — reached outward. Not far. Not with the coastal clarity that had read trajectories from miles away. A few hundred yards. Enough to feel the castle. Enough to feel the boy inside it.

"He's going to Sing," she said.

"Yes."

"He's going to die."

"He's going to become something else. Whether that's death depends on how much help he has."

---

Sereth told her the truth. Quickly. Without the embellishment that three centuries of wearing borrowed faces had taught them. Without the narrative architecture that mortal storytelling required. The facts. The raw, undecorated, devastating facts.

Who they were: one of the seven gods who had created the world's elemental memory. The one who had loved humanity enough to give them the Song. The one who had been punished for the giving by being stripped of divinity and sent to walk among the creatures they had chosen over their own kind.

What magic was: the world's memory, made accessible. Not a force — a conversation. The world remembered everything, and magic was the act of participating in that remembering. The disciplines were languages. The Song was the original language that all the others had been divided from.

Why they had given it: because the creatures were beautiful. Because the creatures loved each other with a ferocity that the gods — infinite, eternal, incapable of loss — could not comprehend. Because the creatures' love was made more intense by their mortality, and the intensity was the most beautiful thing Sereth had ever seen, and the beauty deserved a voice.

What the Song was: love made audible. The harmonization of all seven disciplines producing a frequency that was not magic but the thing magic was made of — the world's memory of being one thing, expressed through a willing vessel.

What it cost: the vessel. The identity. The self, consumed by the harmonization, the individual dissolved into the universal. Fletcher had Sung and ceased. Maera had held five notes and died. The Song demanded everything because the Song was everything.

And what Sereth needed Laine to do: carry them into the Chamber. Because Sereth's nearly-mortal body could not survive the void alone — the Murkr's anti-memory field was stripping the last of their divinity, and without the divinity, the body was failing. But once inside the Chamber, Sereth could serve as a conduit. A bridge between Aelo and the prisoners. The Blood Vine network that Varas had built to drain could be reversed — used not to extract but to distribute, the parasitic architecture repurposed as a delivery system, the Vine carrying the Song from Aelo through Sereth to every prisoner in the Chamber.

"Fletcher Sang alone," Sereth said. "And it cost him everything. The Song consumed him because there was no one to share the cost. No conduit. No distribution. The full weight of seven harmonized disciplines landed on one person and the person was not enough and the person became the Song because the Song needed somewhere to go and the person was the only vessel available."

"And you can change that?"

"I can be a second vessel. I can take some of the Song into myself — channel it through the Blood Vine to the prisoners, to the Stones, to the network. The cost will still be enormous. But it won't be total. The Song will have two vessels instead of one, and the distribution will reduce the load on each."

"Will he survive?"

"He'll survive. But he'll be diminished. The Song will take memories. Identity. The specific, personal, irreplaceable content of his life — the things that make him him. Some will survive. Some won't. He'll live, but he'll live in a world that's slightly less vivid than the one he remembers."

"And you?"

Sereth smiled. The smile was the first real expression the true face had produced — not the fisherman's weathered grin or the old woman's wry half-smile but something older and simpler and carrying the specific, quiet, devastatingly human warmth of a person who has made peace with what's coming and who has discovered, in the making-peace, that peace is not resignation but arrival.

"I've been losing pieces of myself for three centuries. The Murkr is just finishing the job. When the Song passes through me, whatever divinity remains will burn away. I'll become mortal. Fully, permanently, irrevocably mortal. I'll age. I'll weaken. I'll die. Like every other person. Like Essa. Like the creatures I loved enough to ruin myself for."

"That doesn't sound like a cost. That sounds like what you wanted all along."

The golden eye — the last divine feature, the last visible evidence of the god that Sereth had been — held Laine's reassembled gaze. The eye was warm. The eye was the color of the world's first light, and the light was dimming, and the dimming was not loss but completion.

"It is what I wanted all along," Sereth said. "I just needed a reason to let it happen."

They entered the Murkr together. Laine carrying Sereth — the old body lighter than it should have been, the flesh reduced by the divinity's departure to something that was more memory than mass. A human carrying the last god toward the last Song. The grey pressed. The divine memory fell. And Sereth walked into the void with nothing left except the choice and the caring and the specific, mortal, extraordinary stubbornness of a person who had been trying to be a person for three hundred years and who was, at last, about to succeed.
