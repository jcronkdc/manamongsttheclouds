# Chapter 26

## The Training of the Clouds

---

The Seers taught him to hold the world in his mouth.

Not literally — though the physical sensation was close enough that the metaphor survived scrutiny. The Sing was a surrender — the willingness to open every channel at once, to become the junction through which the world's memory passed on its way from silence to song. What the Seers could teach was the approach — the preparation of a mind and a body for an act that would, if it succeeded, consume both.

The training was musical. Literally.

The five Seers sat in a circle on the chamber floor. Each one hummed a note. Each note corresponded to a discipline, the frequency matched to the vibrational signature that the discipline produced in the world's memory.

The eldest Seer — the woman with the milky eyes, whose name Aelo learned was Cael — hummed the Know. A low, sustained drone that lived in the bass register and settled into Aelo's chest before he understood he was hearing it. The vibration moved through his ribs, his sternum, the cavity behind his lungs where the riverbed lived. His body recognized it the way a sleeping person recognizes a familiar voice: without waking, without deciding, the recognition operating beneath thought.

To her left, a man whose face was all angles and weathered bone hummed the Mold. Percussive. Rhythmic. A series of short, sharp exhalations that mimicked the pulse of stone, the heartbeat of the earth. Hit. Hit. Hit. The sound of a material that communicated through pressure rather than pitch.

A woman with her eyes closed took the Heal. Her phrase descended, falling like water, and Aelo felt something loosen in the place where his numbness began — the boundary line between living skin and dead nerve. The loosening was brief. The Heal's frequency passed through the damaged nerves and was gone, but the passage left a warmth that lingered.

A man so thin he seemed to be made of wind raised his hands, palms up, and the air in the chamber shifted. The Move. His hum was a rising phrase, and as it rose, the dust on the chamber floor lifted — a millimeter, no more — the particles suspended in a momentary defiance of gravity that was not a demonstration but a side effect, the discipline leaking into the physical world because the practitioner's body could not contain it.

The last Seer was young. The youngest of the five, her voice clear and strong, she held the Guide: a sustained middle tone, steady as a compass needle, the note that did not rise or fall but pointed.

Five notes. Five disciplines. The chamber filled with them — not as sound but as _structure_. Each note occupied a different register, a different emotional territory, and the combination produced not a chord but a _space_ — an acoustic room in which each voice was distinct and each voice was essential and the absence of any one voice would collapse the structure the way the removal of a single stone collapses an arch.

"Listen," Cael said. She did not stop humming — the word came out layered over the drone, the Know's frequency carrying the instruction as a vibration within a vibration. "Not to the notes. To the space between them."

Aelo listened. The riverbed came — the descent, the settling, the resolved depth that the Ming had taught him. He sank through the five notes the way he had sunk through the lake's memory, past the surface, past the individual voices, into the structure beneath. And there, in the space between the five frequencies, he found it: a gap. A silence. Not the silence of absence but the silence of potential — the charged quiet of a space shaped exactly right for a voice that had not yet spoken.

Two voices were missing. The Burn — the crackling, volatile frequency of fire and transformation, the discipline that the Cloud People did not carry in their blood because the Burn belonged to the Volcano and the Volcano's heat was antithetical to the Cloud's altitude. And the Sing — the voice that lived beneath all the others, the harmonic that was not a discipline but the thing the disciplines were made of.

"Now," Cael said. "Hold one."

He reached for the Know. The low drone was familiar — the riverbed, the Ming's gift. He held it. The drone entered him through the blood, the frequency matching his own, the Know's vibration settling into his body the way the lake stone's stillness settled into his palm.

"Hold two."

He reached for the Mold. The percussive beat was harder — requiring a different part of his attention, a different register, the conviction-based awareness that Torvus had taught him in the Core. He held it. The two frequencies overlapped — the drone and the beat, the listening and the speaking, the Know and the Mold operating simultaneously in his consciousness the way two hands operate simultaneously on a keyboard, each doing something different, the coordination requiring surrender.

Two notes. He had done this before — at the sealed passage, without training, the Know and the Mold harmonizing to produce something that Torvus had said was not Molding but something else. Now he was doing it deliberately. The two notes held. The space between them hummed.

"Three."

The Guide. Laine's discipline — the sustained middle tone, the frequency of momentum. He reached for it and it came easily, too easily, the Guide sense that had been open since the Volcano responding to the Seers' frequency the way a dog responds to its name. Three notes. The drone, the beat, the sustained tone. Three disciplines, held simultaneously, each one drawing on a different part of his memory — the Know on his attention, the Mold on his conviction, the Guide on his perception. Three parts of himself, offered to three different aspects of the world's memory at the same time.

The strain was emotional. Each discipline drew on a different emotional resource, and holding three open simultaneously was like trying to have three conversations at once, each one requiring full engagement, each one pulling his attention in a different direction. The competition produced a tension that lived in his chest — a tightening, a pressure, the sensation of a container being filled beyond its designed capacity.

"Four."

The Heal. Jalo's discipline — the descending phrase, the frequency of mending. He reached for it and the reaching was different. He had not been trained in the Heal. He had no muscle for it, no practice, no teacher who had shown him the approach. But the frequency was in his blood — Jalo's frequency, the guardian's gift, the fifteen years of Healing that Aelo had grown up inside. The Heal was not a skill he had learned. It was a home he had lived in. He reached for it the way you reach for the light switch in a room you know by heart — without looking, without thinking, the body remembering where the switch is because the body has found it ten thousand times in the dark.

Four notes. The pressure in his chest doubled. The strain was significant now — _demanding_, the emotional equivalent of holding a heavy weight at arm's length. Each discipline pulled. Each discipline wanted all of him. And holding four meant giving each one a quarter, and a quarter was not enough for any of them, and the insufficiency produced a wobble — a vibration within the vibration, a tremor in the chord that the Seers could hear and that Cael's milky eyes tracked with the attention of a woman who had been listening for this moment for decades.

"Five."

The Move. The Cloud People's discipline — the rising phrase, the frequency of displacement. He had never been trained in it. He had no connection to it. The Move was the sky's language and Aelo had spent his life on the ground, and the reaching was like trying to speak a language he had never heard by assembling sounds from languages he knew. He reached. He strained. The five notes wobbled — the chord destabilizing, the emotional pressure in his chest becoming a physical sensation, the tightening migrating from his sternum to his throat to his skull.

Five notes. He held them for three seconds. The chamber vibrated. The skystone veins pulsed. The rope bridges outside hummed. The air in the room changed — the density shifting, the atmosphere compressing around him the way the atmosphere compresses before a storm, the world's memory responding to the five-note chord by tightening, by waiting, by preparing for whatever comes next.

He reached for six.

The Burn was not there. The Burn was fire and transformation and the Volcano's heat and the memory of the first flame, and it was not in his blood, and it was not in the sky, and the reaching produced nothing — a grasping at a frequency that did not exist in the space the Seers had created, a hand closing on air.

He screamed.

The five notes collapsed. The chord shattered. The emotional pressure in his chest detonated outward as force, the accumulated strain of five simultaneously held disciplines releasing at once the way a compressed spring releases, the energy dispersing through the chamber in a shockwave that was not physical but emotional. The Seers flinched. Cael's hands flew to her temples. Blood ran from Aelo's nose — thin, bright, the body's declaration that the vessel had been overfilled and the overflow had found the path of least resistance.

He collapsed. The chamber floor caught him — the stone warm from the Move's embedded heat, the skystone veins pulsing with a fading golden light that was the echo of the five-note chord, the residue of a Song that had almost been sung.

The blood dripped. The room settled. The Seers lowered their hands.

Aelo lay on the floor and breathed. Each breath was an effort — the lungs protesting, the chest tight, the body communicating in the unambiguous language of a system that had been pushed past its design parameters and was requesting, firmly, that the pushing stop.

---

Cael came to him that evening. He was sitting on the edge of the formation — legs dangling over the gap, the fog below, the sky above, the thin cold air carrying the Song's frequency as it always did, the sustained hum that had been Aelo's companion since the ascent. His nose had stopped bleeding. His chest still ached.

She sat beside him. The old woman moved with the care of a person whose body was mostly finished with the act of living and who was maintaining the residual functions out of habit and necessity rather than enthusiasm. Her milky eyes turned toward the sky. Her skeletal hands folded in her lap.

"Fletcher held all seven," she said.

"I can hold five. For three seconds."

"Five is extraordinary. No one since your mother has held five. The sixth — the Burn — is not in your blood. You have Know, Mold, Guide, Heal, and Move. Five of seven. Fletcher had all seven because Fletcher was the first, and the first was given all of them, and the giving was not skill but origin."

"How do I have five? I was only trained in the Know and the Mold."

Cael's milky eyes held him with the patient attention of a woman who had been waiting for this question.

"Disciplines arrive through two doors. The first is training — the teacher, the practice, the body learning the shape of the magic through repetition. The Know and the Mold came through that door. But the second door is older: the blood. Your mother was a Cloud Singer. The Move lives in the Cloud bloodline the way the Know lives in the Ming — not as a skill but as a memory, carried in the cells, passed from parent to child. You did not learn the Move. You inherited it. The sky remembered your mother's frequency in your blood, and when you reached this altitude, the blood and the sky recognized each other."

She paused. The wind moved through the chamber, carrying the Song's high frequency.

"The Heal is different. The Heal was not inherited — it was absorbed. Fifteen years inside a Healer's practice. Your guardian mixed the tinctures, prepared the herbs, applied the Ironbark to his own knee every night while you slept in the next room. The Heal operates through proximity as much as through intention — it is the discipline of mending, and mending does not respect walls. Every time your guardian Healed, the frequency passed through the cottage and into you, the way warmth passes through a shared wall. You did not learn the Heal. You grew up inside it."

The explanation settled into Aelo's understanding the way the lake stone settled into his palm — compressed, portable, clarifying. Two doors. One opened by effort; one opened by belonging. The training and the blood. The teacher and the home.

"Then I can't Sing."

"You can't Sing the way Fletcher Sang. Seven notes, all at once, the full harmonization that divided the silence into the seven elements and created the disciplines. That Sing is Fletcher's. It belongs to the first moment. It cannot be repeated because the moment cannot be repeated."

Aelo looked at her. The milky eyes looked back — or past, or through, the opacity seeing something that clarity could not.

"Then what can I do?"

"You can Sing _your_ Song. Not Fletcher's — yours. The Song is not a fixed composition. The Song is the world's memory expressing itself through a willing vessel, and the expression changes with the vessel, the way water changes shape with the container that holds it. Fletcher's Song was the Song of seven elements because Fletcher was the beginning and the beginning required division. Your Song will be different."

"Different how?"

Cael was quiet for a long time. The wind moved. The fog shifted below them, the perpetual concealment of The Refuge responding to air currents that the Move practitioners maintained with the constant, unconscious attention of people whose discipline was so deeply integrated into their lives that the practicing and the living were indistinguishable.

"Fletcher divided," Cael said. "He took the silence and he broke it into seven voices. That was necessary. The world needed the voices. But the breaking had a cost — the voices forgot they were one. Each discipline became separate. Each region became distinct. The world was given seven languages and forgot that they were all dialects of the same tongue."

She looked at him. The milky eyes, for a moment, seemed to clear — the opacity thinning, the dark irises emerging, the depth behind them enormous and ancient and carrying the accumulated wisdom of a woman who had been listening to the Song for longer than Aelo had been alive.

"Your Song will not divide. Your Song will _remind_. The way you reminded the pebble of its round shape. The way you reminded the sealed passage of its openness. The way you reminded the sky of your mother's voice. You don't break. You restore. You don't create new voices. You remind the existing voices that they are one."

"What does that require?"

"Everything Fletcher required. Your self. Your identity. The complete surrender of everything you are to the act of harmonization. The difference is: Fletcher surrendered into the Song and became the Song. You will surrender into the memory and become the _reminder_. Not a note — a resonance. Not a sound — a recognition."

"Will I die?"

The question was the question he had been carrying since the elder woman at the Ming had said _gift or death sentence_. Since Laine had refused to describe the third future. Since the first morning the Song had arrived in his blood and the blood had recognized it and the recognition had been both the most beautiful and the most terrifying thing he had ever felt.

Cael paused. The pause was not hesitation. It was the weighted pause of a person choosing between the merciful lie and the devastating truth and deciding, as the Ming had decided, as Jalo had decided, as every person who had ever loved Aelo had decided: the truth.

"Fletcher did not die," she said. "He became the Song. Whether that is death depends on what you believe death is."

"What do you believe?"

"I believe that your mother has been singing in this sky for fifteen years." The milky eyes held him. "Draw your own conclusions."

Aelo sat on the edge of the formation and looked at the sky and thought about his mother singing in the air and Fletcher singing in the stone and the Desert elder singing to the sand and the Weave-Roads singing beneath the feet of people who had learned to braid weakness into strength. He thought about Torvus singing to rock with numb hands. About the Ming teacher singing to silence with an expressionless face. About Jalo singing to a boy every morning for fifteen years with a bottle of herbs and a bowl of porridge and the devastating, inarticulable music of showing up.

The Song was the act of reminding the world that it was one thing — that the seven voices were one voice, that the seven elements were one element, that the silence and the sound were not opposites but aspects of the same presence, the way the ocean and the rain and the tears on a boy's face were all, at some depth, the same water.

He could not hold seven notes. He could hold five. And beneath the five — unnamed, untrained, growing — the sixth and the seventh were not absent. They were waiting. The Burn was in the blade he had felt in the Volcano, the transformation of ore into weapon, the fire's memory of change. The Sing was in everything — in the sky, in the stone, in the blood, in the frequency that his mother had left in the air and that his father had left in his eyes and that Jalo had left in every morning of his life.

He did not need to hold all seven. He needed to remind the world that all seven were already held. That they had always been held. That the holding was not a performance but a memory, and the memory was not lost but buried.

He stood. He walked back to the chamber. Cael followed, slowly, the old body maintaining itself through habit. The milky eyes fixed on the boy who was walking with the quiet certainty of a person who has understood something and is now carrying the understanding like a stone in his hand: compressed, portable, always accessible.

"I know what my Song is," he said.

Cael waited. The wind waited. The Clouds waited.

"I'm going to remind the world that it's one thing. Not by Singing louder. By listening deeper. The riverbed. The place where all the voices merge. I've been there. The Ming showed me. The riverbed is where the Song lives — not above the voices but _beneath_ them. If I can reach it — if I can hold the riverbed and let the voices come through me the way they come through the Elder Stones — I don't need to hold seven notes. I need to hold _one_. The one that all seven are made of."

Cael's face changed. The milky eyes widened — a fractional adjustment, the muscles around the eyes contracting, the opacity seeming, for a moment, to vanish entirely. What emerged behind it was not vision but understanding — the rare, irreplaceable understanding of a woman who has been waiting for an answer and has received one she did not expect and that is better than the one she hoped for.

"Fletcher divided the silence into seven voices," she said. "And you will remind the seven voices that they are silence."

"Not silence. Stillness. The stillness that holds the Song. The riverbed."

Cael smiled. The smile was thin — the muscles too old, the face too weathered to produce the full expression. But the smile was real. The first real smile Aelo had seen on a Seer's face.

"Your mother would be proud," she said. "Your mother _is_ proud. I can hear her in the air right now. She's been singing louder since you arrived."

Aelo listened. The sky hummed. The Song was there — not beneath, not above, but everywhere. In the stone and the air and the fog and the wind and the bridges and the birds and the old woman's thin smile and the stars that were appearing, one by one, in the darkening sky above the Clouds.

He was not ready. But he knew what his Song was. And the knowing was the beginning of the readiness, the way the riverbed was the beginning of the listening, the way morning begins the day.

Tomorrow, he would descend. Tomorrow, the last leg. Tomorrow, the Murkr.

Tonight, he listened. And the sky sang back.
