# Chapter 39

## The Conduit

---

Sereth reached the Chamber on Laine's back, and the Chamber remembered her.

Not the Blood Vine — the Blood Vine recoiled from the divine residue that still clung to Sereth's diminished body the way static clings to cloth, the organic tendrils curling away from the presence that predated them by millennia. Not the Elder Stones — the Stones hummed with recognition, the sickly amber light brightening for a moment, the stolen memories inside them stirring at the proximity of the being who had created the original Stones, who had designed the crystalline structure that stored the world's memory, who had given Fletcher the knowledge of how to pour a life into a mineral lattice and make it sing. The stone remembered. The walls, the floor, the ceiling — the original Fletcher-era masonry, emerging from beneath the Blood Vine's corruption — felt Sereth's presence and responded with the specific, deep, geological recognition of a material encountering the hand that had first shaped it. Not Sereth's hand — not personally. But the divine frequency. The god-signature. The vibration that the stone had been designed to resonate with and that it had been waiting three centuries to feel again.

Laine set Sereth down. Gently — the old body was fragile now, the divinity stripped by the Murkr's anti-memory field, the flesh reduced to mortal parameters. Sereth's golden eye — the last divine feature — was dimming. The amber that had once been the color of the world's first light was fading toward brown, the divine pigment converting to human pigment, the god becoming a person in the specific, visual, pigment-by-pigment transition that mortality required.

She stood. The standing cost her — the legs trembling, the balance uncertain, the body operating on the residual architecture of three centuries of practice rather than the divine vitality that had sustained it. She looked around the Chamber.

The prisoners. The rows of platforms. The open eyes that saw nothing. The Blood Vine connecting each person to each Stone, the parasitic network pulsing with its slow, rhythmic draw.

Sereth's face — the true face, the one that was neither old nor young nor human nor god but something in between — produced an expression that Laine, standing beside her, had never seen on any face. Not grief. Not rage. Not the specific, architectural emotions that the disciplines produced. Something older. Something that came from a time before emotions had names. The expression of a being that had given humanity the most powerful gift in the universe and was now seeing what humanity had done with it.

"I did this," Sereth said. The voice was barely audible — the thin, reduced voice of a nearly-mortal being speaking through a throat that was losing its divine resonance. "I gave Fletcher the Song. I showed him how to make the Stones. I designed the architecture that stores memory in crystal. Every Stone in this room, every Vine that grows from it, every draining mechanism that Varas built — it's all based on what I gave to a man who wanted to help the world remember."

"You couldn't have known."

"I knew. I knew that every gift could be a weapon. I knew that the capacity to remember could become the capacity to steal. I gave it anyway because the alternative was silence, and silence was worse." She looked at the prisoners — the rows of almost-empty people, each one holding a single memory like a candle in the dark. "The silence was worse. It is still worse. Even this — even the Chamber and the draining and the seventy years of consumption — is not as bad as the silence would have been. Because the silence would have been permanent, and this —" She gestured at the room. At the Vine. At the Stones. At the prisoners. "This can be undone."

She began unwinding the Blood Vine from the prisoners.

Not with magic — with her hands. The nearly-mortal hands, the fingers that had lost their divine strength and that now operated at human capacity, the grip measured in human force. She took the first tendril — the one connected to the woman on the fourth platform, the woman whose last memory was a first kiss — and she pulled. Gently. Carefully. The way you remove a thorn from a child's palm: with attention, with patience, with the specific, unhurried tenderness of a person who understands that the removal must not cause more damage than the thorn itself.

The Vine resisted. The organic growth had been attached for years — had grown into the skin, had threaded through the nerve pathways, had established the connection between flesh and Stone that the draining required. Pulling it free was not cutting — it was _unweaving_. Each tendril had to be separated from the tissue it had penetrated, the organic filaments extracted from the neural pathways they had colonized, the parasitic connection severed at the cellular level.

Sereth worked. Laine joined — her hands steadier than Sereth's, her Guide-damaged eyes providing a clarity that the divine eye could not, the human vision seeing the physical details that the god's vision had always processed as frequencies rather than forms. Together, they worked through the row — prisoner by prisoner, tendril by tendril, the unweaving slow and precise and carrying, in its deliberate pace, the specific quality of an act of restoration rather than an act of violence.

Each freed prisoner gasped. The gasp was involuntary — the body's response to the sudden cessation of the drain, the specific, physical, whole-body shudder of a system that had been losing something for years and that felt, in the moment the losing stopped, the full weight of everything that had been lost. The single memory flared — the first kiss, the child's laugh, the bread, the rain — each one brightening for a moment, the candle in the dark guttering and then steadying, the flame small but stable, the fuel no longer being consumed.

The prisoners were not restored. The draining had taken too much. The single memories remained — small, fragile, the compressed residue of lives that had been full and were now almost empty. But they were disconnected. Free. For the first time in years, no one was pulling at what little they had left.

---

Sereth connected the Blood Vine to herself.

The act was deliberate. She took the ends of the severed tendrils — the dozens of connections that had been feeding from the prisoners — and she gathered them. One by one. Each tendril placed against her own skin — her chest, her arms, the places where the flesh was thinnest and the divine residue was strongest. The Vine, which had been designed to connect to living tissue and draw memory through the connection, attached. The tendrils penetrated. The filaments threaded. The connection established.

The sensation was — Sereth breathed through it. The Vine's draw was immediate, automatic, the parasitic system engaging the moment the connection was established. But Sereth was not a prisoner. Sereth was a god — or the remnant of one — and the divine memory that the Vine found when it began to draw was not the personal, human memory of a life lived in time. It was the foundational memory. The memory of the world's beginning. The memory that predated the elements and the disciplines and the Song itself.

The Vine drew. Sereth held. The draw met the holding and the two forces balanced — the parasitic pull against the divine resistance, the consumption against the sustaining, the taking against the giving. The balance was not comfortable. The balance was the specific, sustained, agonizing equilibrium of two opposing forces meeting at the exact point where neither could overcome the other.

"What are you doing?" Laine asked. Her voice was tight — the voice of a woman watching something she did not fully understand and recognizing, through the damaged Guide's residual trajectory-sense, that the something was irreversible.

"Becoming a bridge. When the boy Sings, the Song needs to reach everyone in this room — every prisoner, every Stone, every thread. My body — what's left of my divinity — can carry it. The Blood Vine network that Varas built to drain can be reversed. I can amplify the note. Spread it. Make sure it reaches every person the Vine touches."

"What will it cost you?"

"Everything I was." The golden eye — dimming, the amber fading, the divine pigment dissolving into human brown — found Laine's face. "But I haven't been a god in a very long time. I've been a person who remembers being one. Now I'll just be a person."

The smile came. The first real smile — not the fisherman's weathered grin or the old woman's wry half-smile or any of the borrowed expressions that three centuries of borrowed faces had produced. Sereth's smile. The god's smile. The expression of a being that had been trying to be mortal for three hundred years and that was, in this moment, about to succeed. The smile transformed the true face — the neither-old-nor-young, neither-human-nor-god face becoming, for one moment, the face of a person who was at peace with what was coming because what was coming was what she had always wanted.

"I chose this," Sereth said. "Three hundred years ago, I chose to love the creatures. The other six said the loving would destroy me. They were right. The loving destroyed the god. But it made the person. And the person —" She looked at the Chamber. At the prisoners. At the Vine in her chest. At the Stones pulsing in the walls. "The person is enough. The person was always enough. I just needed three hundred years to believe it."

She turned to the locked door. Aldric. Vero. She opened it — with her own hand, because the stone remembered _her_. The stone was younger than Sereth. The stone had been quarried from mountains that Sereth had walked when the mountains were hills. The mechanism engaged at the touch of the divine hand — the original hand, the hand that had designed the mechanism, the hand that had shown Fletcher how to build the frequency locks that keyed to the bloodline.

She touched Aldric's face. The bark-free, Vine-withered face of the king who had been aware for thirty years. The king's eyes focused — the first time in three decades that the eyes had focused on a face that was not his captor's. What Aldric saw was not a face. It was a presence. The god who had started everything. The god who had given Fletcher the gift that had produced the bloodline that had produced the family that had produced the son who had consumed the world.

"I'm sorry, old friend," Sereth said. "I should have come sooner."

Aldric's lips moved. One word, barely audible, produced by a throat that had not spoken in thirty years and that had to remember, muscle by muscle, how to shape air into sound: "Finally."

Sereth connected the Blood Vine from Aldric and Vero to the network — to herself, to the conduit she was building. When the Song came, it would reach them too. The king and the prince. The father who had failed and the brother who had pleaded. The Song would reach them and the Song would remind them and the reminding would be the beginning of whatever came next.

---

Aelo entered the Chamber.

He came through the door that led from the corridors — the corridors that had straightened, the stone that had remembered, the castle that was slowly, under the Song's resonance, returning to the home it had been before the monster made it a mouth. He stood in the doorway and he saw: Sereth at the center, wrapped in Blood Vine, the golden eye nearly brown, the true face drawn and pale and human. The prisoners on their platforms, disconnected, their single memories flickering. Aldric and Vero in the locked room, connected to the network through Sereth's conduit. Laine at the door, tears on her face, the damaged Guide providing a clarity that was not trajectory-sight but something else — the human sight, the simple, unaugmented, mortal capacity to see what is in front of you and understand that it matters.

He walked to the center. He stood beside Sereth. The god-turned-person. The being who had given humanity the gift and was now, in the final act, giving the last of herself to ensure that the gift's greatest expression would not destroy the boy who carried it.

"I don't know how to Sing," Aelo said. The words were honest. The words were the words of a boy who had been trained in five disciplines and had held five notes and had heard the full seven-voice chord in his blood and who still, in this moment, standing in the Chamber beside a dying god and surrounded by the victims of a king who could not hear — still did not know how to produce the sound that the world was waiting for.

Sereth looked at him. The golden-brown eye — nearly human now, the divinity almost gone, the god almost finished becoming the person — held the boy's face with the specific, accumulated, three-century depth of a being that had been watching over Fletcher's descendants since the first one was born and that had watched them fail and die and try again and fail again and that had, through three hundred years of failure, never stopped believing that one of them would succeed.

"Yes you do," Sereth said. "You've been Singing your whole life. Every time you felt someone's pain. Every time you heard Jalo's nightmares through the wall. Every time you cried over a dead bird in the yard. The Song isn't a technique. It's love made audible. You've been doing it since you were born."

The words settled in the Chamber. The Blood Vine pulsed — the network awaiting the signal, the conduit ready, the bridge between the boy and the prisoners and the Stones and the world built and waiting. The prisoners' single memories flickered — the kiss, the laugh, the bread, the rain — each one a candle, each one waiting for the wind that would not blow them out but fan them into something brighter.

Everything was ready. The conduit was set. The prisoners were disconnected from Varas and connected to Sereth. Sereth was connected to everything.

Laine stood at the door. Her damaged eyes — the Guide-sight fractured, the restless trajectory-reading replaced by a human stillness that was, in its own way, more perceptive than the Guide had ever been — watched the boy and the god and the Chamber and the convergence of every trajectory she had ever read. Everything — every thread of every life in this room — was converging on a single point. A single note.

She closed her eyes. She did not pray — Laine did not pray. She did something harder. She believed. She believed in the boy who had no plan and the god who had spent three centuries becoming a person and the Song that was love made audible and the world that was waiting to be reminded.

Sereth felt the believing. The nearly-human heart, beating in the nearly-mortal chest, felt it the way it had felt Essa's believing three hundred years ago — the specific, warm, devastating quality of a human choosing to trust another human, the bridge that Varas had been born without and that Sereth had spent three centuries trying to build and that was, in this moment, present in a room full of almost-empty people and flickering memories and a boy who was about to Sing.

_I believe in you instead,_ Sereth thought. _That's always been enough._

The Chamber waited. The conduit hummed. The boy stood at the center.

The Song was about to begin.
