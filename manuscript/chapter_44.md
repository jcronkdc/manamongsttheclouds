# Chapter 44

## The Unfinished Sentence

---

Vero was planting seeds in a garden that did not exist yet.

The patch of earth behind the castle — dead for decades, the soil grey, the surface cracked into the specific, dry, geometric pattern of ground that had forgotten how to hold water — had been cleared. Not by Molders or Move practitioners or any of the magical disciplines that were slowly reactivating in the convergence point as the Song's resonance continued its work. By hand. Vero's hands. The prince who had been dreaming for thirty years had woken and had walked to the back of the castle and had knelt in the dead earth and had begun, with his bare fingers, to dig.

His hands shook. The decades of draining had aged him — the body that had been young and strong when Varas sealed him in the alcove was now old, the joints swollen, the muscles atrophied, the skin loose on a frame that had been maintained by herbs rather than by life. He was sixty years old in a body that looked eighty, and the body's hands trembled when they dug, and the digging was slow, and the slowness was not a problem because Vero was not in a hurry. Vero had spent thirty years in a hurry — thirty years of trying to finish a sentence, the words repeating, the plea cycling, the dream locked in the specific, agonizing urgency of a man who needs to say something and cannot stop trying. The hurry was finished. The sentence was finished. What remained was the opposite of hurry: the patient, deliberate, unhurried act of putting seeds in the ground and trusting the ground to do the rest.

The seeds had come from the Cloud resistance — brought down from the Refuge in sacks that smelled of altitude and cold air. High-altitude seeds, adapted for thin soil and harsh conditions. Not the lush, temperamental varieties that the Canopy's rich earth could produce. Hardy things. Stubborn things. Seeds that had been designed by generations of Cloud agriculture to grow in places where growing was difficult, because the Cloud People understood that difficulty was not an obstacle to growth but a condition of it.

Aelo found him there in the afternoon. The prince was on his knees in the dead earth, his shaking hands pressing seeds into shallow holes, his face carrying the specific, concentrated, entirely-absorbed expression of a person engaged in an act that required no skill and total attention.

"You look like Maera," Vero said. He did not look up. His hands continued their work — seed, hole, cover, move. The rhythm was meditative. The rhythm was the opposite of the dream's cycling urgency. "Around the eyes. She had those eyes — the specific, attentive, slightly-too-wide eyes of a person who is seeing more than the visible. The Know eyes. Your mother had them. You have them."

Aelo sat beside him. The dead earth was cool — not the Murkr's former cold, which had been the absence of warmth, but the natural cool of soil that was beginning to remember how to regulate its own temperature. He watched the prince plant. The shaking hands. The careful placement. The trust.

"Tell me about him," Aelo said. "Not the king. The boy."

Vero's hands paused. The pause was not the Ming's weighted interval or the guardian's defensive silence. It was the pause of a man selecting a memory from among many — the memories restored by the Song, the thirty years of consumed experience returned in the flood, the prince's life given back to him in fragments that he was still assembling into a coherent narrative.

"Varas at four," Vero said. "Trying to build a tower from stones. In the garden — this garden, before it died. He would stack the stones and they would fall and he would stack them again. Over and over. He never asked for help. He never cried when they fell. He just — rebuilt. Patiently. Methodically. As if the falling was not failure but data, and the data was informing the next attempt."

He placed a seed. His hands resumed their rhythm.

"Varas at seven. Sitting outside our father's study. Our father was inside, laughing with the Minister of the Canopy — I could hear them through the door, and the laughing was warm, the way laughing is warm when the people producing it are connected by the Know and the connection amplifies the joy and the amplified joy amplifies the laughter. A feedback loop of warmth. Varas was sitting outside the door. On the floor. Listening. And his face — his face was the face of a child trying to solve a puzzle that has no solution. He could hear the laughter. He could not feel why it was happening. The laughter was a sound without a cause, the way a word in a foreign language is a sound without a meaning."

Another seed. Another hole. The rhythm continued — the prince's hands moving through the dead earth with the deliberate, patient motion of a man who had decided that the planting was the most important thing he could do and who was not going to stop until the planting was done.

"Varas at twelve. He came to me — to my room, late at night, which was unusual because Varas did not come to people. Varas waited for people to come to him, because the coming was the only evidence he had that he mattered, and evidence collected passively was the only kind that did not require the bridge he did not have." Vero paused. "He sat on my bed and he asked me: 'What does the music sound like?'"

"The Song?"

"Not the Song specifically. Everything. The emotional music — the ambient Know that every person carries, the low-grade, unconscious, always-present awareness of other people's feelings that we all experience and that he did not. He wanted to know what it sounded like. What it felt like. What it was like to walk into a room and feel the room feeling you."

"What did you tell him?"

Vero's hands stopped. The shaking intensified — not from the effort of digging but from the effort of remembering. The memory was restored. The memory was vivid. The memory was one of the heaviest things the flood had returned.

"I didn't know how to answer. I was twelve. How do you describe sound to someone who's never heard it? How do you describe color to someone who's never seen it? I said — I said something stupid. I said: 'It's like being warm.' And he looked at me and said: 'I know what warm is. Warm is sixty-two degrees.' And I realized that he was not asking because he was curious. He was asking because he was _trying_. He was trying to understand what he could not feel by translating it into what he could feel. Sixty-two degrees. The temperature of a Stone's surface. That was his entire emotional vocabulary. Temperature. Measurement. Data."

Aelo sat with this. The image of a twelve-year-old boy on his brother's bed, asking what music sounded like and receiving an answer that was as inadequate as every answer would be, because the question was not about information but about experience, and experience could not be transmitted, only shared, and sharing required the bridge that the boy did not have.

"I failed him," Vero said. "Everyone failed him. We saw the void and we backed away from it instead of filling it. That's what you do with a void — you fill it. With patience, with presence, with the specific, stubborn, daily practice of being near someone who cannot feel you and being near them anyway. We just... left him empty."

"Could anything have filled it? Was he born without the ability to hear, or was it something that could have been taught?"

Vero planted another seed. The question lived in the air between them — the most important question, the question that reframed everything, the question that determined whether the Chamber and the draining and the Dog Scene were the inevitable consequence of a birth defect or the avoidable consequence of a failure of love.

"I don't know," Vero said. "Our father — our father Knew Varas. Felt the void. And our father's response was to protect himself and protect me and leave Varas standing outside the door listening to laughter he could not feel. If our father had knelt beside that four-year-old and held him and said — not _I can feel your pain_, because our father could not feel what was not there — but _I see that you cannot feel, and I will stay with you anyway_ — if our father had done that, would the void have filled? Would the bridge have built itself? Would the music have arrived?

He looked at Aelo. The restored eyes — old in a young man's skull, the decades of draining visible as a depth that age alone could not produce — held the specific, devastating, impossible-to-answer clarity of a man who had spent thirty years dreaming the same dream and who had woken to discover that the dream's question was the only question that mattered.

"I don't know. And that's the cruelest part. We'll never know because no one tried."

---

Vero finished planting. He stood — the knees cracking, the body protesting, the effort of rising from the ground producing the specific, audible, mechanical objections of joints that had been immobile for thirty years and that were rediscovering the concept of motion. He looked at the garden. The dead earth. The invisible seeds. The trust.

"I wasn't finished speaking to my brother," he said. "The night he drained me. I was going to say: 'Let me help you hear it.' That's all. Let me help. I was going to offer to share my magic — freely, willingly, the way it was meant to be shared. I was going to put my hands on his temples and let him feel what I felt. Not the Know — not the clinical, diagnostic, Ming-style reading. The sharing. The specific, willing, undefended act of opening your own experience to another person and letting them inhabit it."

"Would it have worked?"

"I'll never know. But I think he wanted it to. I think that's why he drained me before I could finish. Because if I'd offered and it didn't work, then there was no hope. And hope — even false hope — was the only thing keeping him alive." He paused. "So he took my memories instead of my offer. Because the memories could be consumed, and the offer could only be accepted or rejected, and rejection was the one thing his void could not survive."

Aelo left Vero in his garden. The seeds would not bloom for months. The dead earth would take years to fully recover — the Song's reminder working through the soil the way the Desert's Weave-Roads worked through the sand, slowly, patiently, one grain at a time.

But the seeds were planted. And the planting was the beginning. And the beginning was enough.
