# Chapter 15

## The Departure

---

The teacher woke him before dawn.

Not with a word — with a shift in the air, a change in the room's emotional weather so subtle that only a boy who had spent two weeks training his attention to distinguish between the weight of one silence and the weight of another would have noticed it. The blue-green moss-light held its steady glow. The bowl of lake water on the shelf trembled with the same subterranean frequency that had been Aelo's companion since the first morning. But there was something else in the room now — a presence that carried the urgency that the Ming expressed not through haste but through density. The teacher stood in the doorway, his expressionless face holding its equilibrium, and the urgency was in his body, in the way his weight was distributed — forward, on the balls of his feet, a posture that said _now_ without the indignity of saying it aloud.

Aelo sat up. His body cooperated — the new body, the longer one, the one that had been assembling itself from the raw materials of unsuppressed growth for weeks now, the shoulders broader, the wrists thicker, the voice settled into a register that no longer surprised him every time he used it. The body cooperated the way a newly built thing cooperates: willingly, but without the ease of long inhabitation. He was still learning the distances — elbow to wall, knee to floor, the revised geometry of a frame that had been compressed for fifteen years .

"Something has changed," the teacher said.

Aelo reached for the riverbed. It came faster now — the descent through layers of noise, the settling past the surface quiet into the resolved depth where individual voices merged into structure. He had been practicing every morning and every evening, the skill strengthening the way the teacher had designed it to strengthen: through failure, through patience, through the accumulated muscle of attention built rep by rep in the blue-green silence of the Ming lake.

He reached the bottom. The Elder Stone's tone was there — sustained, luminous, the sound of sacrifice singing to itself in the dark. But layered over it, like silt stirred from a lake bed by a sudden current: a disturbance. Something moving through the world's memory at speed, leaving a wake of agitation in the emotional weather that Aelo could feel the way a spider feels vibration in its web.

"Something is looking for me," Aelo said.

"Something has been looking for you since you arrived. The lake's density has masked your presence — the Elder Stone's memory is so vast that your signature is lost inside it, the way a single voice is lost in a choir." The teacher paused. The pause was not hesitation — it was the Ming pause, the deliberate space between one truth and the next, the interval that allowed the listener to metabolize what had been said before being asked to swallow what was coming. "The mask is failing. Your signature has grown. What was a voice is becoming a chorus, and the lake cannot contain a chorus."

"How long?"

"Days. Perhaps less. The creature that hunts you — the animal with the stone fused to its skull — is in the Canopy still. But it is moving fast, and what it follows is not scent. It follows the memory of your magic in the earth and the air and the water. Every time you practice, every time you reach the riverbed, you leave a mark in the world's memory. The marks are growing brighter."

The information arrived with a weight that settled in Aelo's chest — not panic, not exactly, but the specific heaviness of a boy who has just been told that the thing that makes him extraordinary is also the thing that will bring the hunters to his door.
"You must leave the Ming," the teacher said.

---

He found Jalo on the southern terrace. The man who had spent seven days being unburdened sat in the grey pre-dawn with a cup of the monastery's root tea balanced on his good knee and his face unwrapped and his eyes open to the lake, and he was — Aelo felt it through the Know before he saw it on the face — already aware. The copper-guilt and the smoke-fear were there, faint but irreducible, the permanent substrata of Halvar Eld's emotional architecture. But the ratios had changed. The hope that Aelo had first detected on the fourth day of training was stronger now, reinforced by a week of shared pain and stories and tinctures made side by side and the radical, unfamiliar experience of being helped. And layered over the hope: readiness. The particular frequency of a soldier who has been resting and knows the rest is ending and is — not eager, not relieved, but _prepared_ — in the way that only someone who has been a soldier can be prepared, which is to say: already grieving the peace while it is still happening.

"I know," Jalo said, before Aelo could speak. "I felt it three hours ago. The Flash Rat. Its stone resonates — a thin, diseased frequency, like an Elder Stone singing through a cracked throat. It's closer than it should be."

"The teacher says days."

"The teacher has never been hunted by the Mrak Patrol." Jalo took a sip of the tea. His hands — the fingers still numb from the Mold, the grip compensated by habit, the cup held between palms rather than pinched by fingertips — were steady. Steadier than Aelo had ever seen them. The tremor that had been Jalo's constant companion for fifteen years was not gone, but it had diminished from a vibration to a whisper, the phantom pain redistributed across a dozen Ming practitioners who carried fractions of it in their own bodies and who would, the elder woman had told him, continue to carry those fractions indefinitely, because the Ming did not consider the bearing of another person's suffering to be a burden but a practice. An exercise in the specific discipline of compassion that required weight the way strength requires resistance.

"Where do we go?" Aelo asked.

"South. The Core first — Torvus is there, and the underground hides signatures. Then the Sea." Jalo looked at him. Both eyes — the clear one and the lidded one — held the same expression: the assessment of a guardian measuring the distance between where his charge is and where his charge needs to be. "You're not ready for any of it. But the hunters don't care about readiness."

---

The elder woman came to them at the shore. Behind her, two monks carrying a wooden case — small, plain, the dark wood polished by generations of handling. The mist was thicker than usual, the lake wrapped in a white quiet that muffled the water's lapping against the monastery's submerged foundations and turned the world into a space of diminished edges, where one thing blurred into the next and the boundaries between stone and water and air became suggestions rather than facts.

"We have something for the boy," the elder woman said.

She opened the case. Inside, resting on a bed of grey silk: a stone. Small — small enough to fit in a closed fist — and polished to a smoothness that was not manufactured but earned, the surface worn by the patient, ceaseless attention of water that had been remembering for three hundred years. It was dark — the grey-green of the lake's deepest water — and when Aelo looked at it, his Know registered a density that should not have been possible in an object that size. As if the stone were heavier on the inside than on the outside. As if it contained more than it displaced.

"From the lake bottom," the teacher said. He stood behind the elder woman, his expressionless face holding the particular stillness that Aelo had learned to read as significance — the absence of expression that paradoxically conveyed more than expression could. "It has rested above the Elder Stone since before the monastery was built. The lake's memory is in it — three hundred years of listening, of stillness, of the specific quiet that exists at the bottom of deep water."

Aelo took the stone. The moment his fingers closed around it, the riverbed came to him — not the effortful descent of the training sessions, not the settling through layers of noise, but an instant arrival, as if the stone were a door and his hand on it was the key. The resolved depth. The luminous tone. The silence that was not absence but structure. It filled him the way warmth fills a cold room, and the noise of the world — the lake, the mist, the monks' heartbeats, Jalo's complex emotional weather, the distant agitated wake of the hunting creature — moved to the far side of a boundary and stayed there, held at bay by three centuries of accumulated stillness compressed into a piece of stone the size of a plum.

"When the noise becomes too much," the teacher said, "hold this. It will remind you of the riverbed."

Aelo closed his hand. The riverbed held. The stone was warm — the faint, internal warmth of a thing that remembered being part of something larger, the way a coal remembers the fire, the way a word remembers the sentence it was spoken in.

"Thank you," he said. The words were inadequate — two syllables for two weeks of silence and failure and patience and the slow, difficult, essential work of learning to listen without drowning. But the teacher's face, for the second time since Aelo had known it, held an expression. Not the wonder-and-fear of the first time. Something quieter. Something that looked, if Aelo's Know was reading it correctly, like the satisfaction of a man who has spent three hundred years listening to a song he cannot understand and has just given a piece of that song to the boy who might.

"Do not thank me," the teacher said. "Come back and Sing."

---

They left the Ming at midmorning, when the mist burned thin enough to see the path that wound south from the monastery into the low hills beyond the lake. Aelo carried the stone in his left hand — the hand with the mark, the amber pulse faint in the daylight, the stone's cool weight a counterpoint to the mark's warmth. Jalo walked beside him with his staff and his newly lightened pain and the expression on his unwrapped face that Aelo was still learning to read: the expression of a man who had been given a week of peace and was storing it the way a camel stores water, in the deep reservoirs of the body, against the drought ahead.

At the monastery gate — a stone arch set into the building's southern wall, low enough that Jalo had to duck — the elder woman placed her palm on Jalo's temple. A brief, final sharing. Aelo felt it through the Know: a last redistribution, the woman taking a measure of the guardian's accumulated phantom suffering and settling it into her own body with the quiet competence of a woman who had been carrying pieces of other people's pain for so long that the carrying had become a form of breathing.

"The road south passes through the Canopy's edge," the elder woman said. She was speaking to Jalo, but her eyes moved to Aelo and held him with the volumetric gaze that the Ming used when they were seeing more than the surface — the expanded pupils, the depthless black, the quality of looking that felt less like observation and more like attendance. "There is a shaft at the Canopy's southern border — an entrance to the Core, old, built before Varas. The Molder will find you there."

"Torvus," Jalo said. Not a question. The name carried the weight of history — a man Jalo knew, had known, from the time before the burning and the baby and the fifteen years of exile.

"He has been told." The elder woman paused. "He was pleased. He said to tell you that you still owe him a drink."

Something happened on Jalo's face — the scarred side pulled, the muscles rearranging around the ridged tissue, producing a shape that was not quite a smile but was in the same territory. The territory of a man remembering a friendship that had survived decades of silence because the friendship was built on a foundation deeper than contact, the way roots survive winter by going below the frost line.

"I owe him several," Jalo said.

They walked south. The monastery receded behind them — the stone walls descending into the mist, the blue-green glow of the moss fading, the lake's vast memory diminishing with distance from a choir to a chorus to a hymn to, eventually, a single sustained note that Aelo could hold in his chest the way the stone held the riverbed: compressed, portable, always accessible.

He did not look back. He was learning this from Jalo — the discipline of forward motion, the specific courage required to leave a place that has been good to you without turning to measure the size of the leaving.

---

From the hilltop, the boy and the guardian looked like ants.

Sereth had left the Ming on the second day — slipped out through the monastery's eastern gate while the boy trained at the lake's edge and the guardian knelt under the elder woman's hands. The Ming had not tried to stop her. The Ming never tried to stop anyone; they simply knew, and the knowing was its own farewell. Sereth had walked south alone, wearing the old woman's body for the last time, and had waited on this hilltop for two weeks — watching, orbiting, doing what she had done for three centuries: attending from a distance that was close enough to intervene and far enough to deny involvement.

Now Sereth watched them descend the southern path — two figures, one tall and limping and carrying the weight of the world on a bad knee, one taller than he had been two weeks ago and carrying a stone in his left hand that pulsed faintly in the Know-spectrum with the compressed memory of three centuries of lake water. They moved well together. The guardian's stride had lengthened since the Ming — the pain-sharing had given him back inches of motion that years of phantom accumulation had stolen — and the boy matched him step for step with the unconscious coordination of someone who had spent fifteen years calibrating his pace to another person's limitations.

They would reach the Canopy's southern edge by nightfall. The shaft to the Core was there — old, overgrown, the entrance concealed by a century of neglect and the Canopy's insistent reclamation of anything built by hands. Torvus would be waiting at the bottom, enormous and boisterous and numb from the shoulders down, ready to teach the boy to speak to stone.

Sereth shifted her weight on the rock she was sitting on. The old woman's body — the broad-shouldered Canopy herbalist, the one-eyed face with its map of sun-lines and forest-squint — was beginning to feel wrong. Not uncomfortable — Sereth had worn bodies the way other people wore clothes for three centuries, and discomfort was not the issue. The issue was resonance. Each body she wore carried a frequency — a signature in the world's memory, a specific way of being perceived by the stones and the soil and the water that remembered everything. The old woman's frequency was Canopy: wood and whisper and the green-dark patience of ancient trees. Where Sereth was going, that frequency would be wrong. The Sea did not speak wood. The Sea spoke salt and tide and the vast, depthless grammar of water that had been the beginning of everything.

She closed her eyes. She breathed. And she remembered.

It was not magic — or rather, it was the oldest magic, older than the disciplines, older than Fletcher, older than the Song. It was the remnant of what she had been before the curse, before the descent, before the other six had stripped her down to almost-mortal and sent her to walk among the creatures she had loved too much. A god could be anything, because a god was the memory of everything. Sereth was no longer a god — the divinity had been leaking out of her for three centuries, a slow diminishment, a slope she could feel herself sliding down with each decade. But the ability to remember a different body — to reach into the vast, fading archive of what she had been and select a different shape the way a musician selects a different instrument — that remained. Diminished. Costly. Each change took more than the last, and each new body fit a little less perfectly than the one before, the edges slightly blurred, the performance a fraction less convincing.

But it held. It still held.

The old woman dissolved. Not dramatically — not the theatrical transformation of the stories that mortals told about shapeshifters, all smoke and light and the crack of bones rearranging. More like a sentence being revised. The same words, rearranged. The same letters, respaced. A middle-aged man emerged from the revision — lean, sun-weathered, with the dark skin and salt-stiffened hair of a fisherman who had spent decades on the water. One eye — the left was gone, scarred over, a detail Sereth carried from body to body because the loss was real and could not be revised away. The remaining eye was brown, deep, carrying the depth that every body Sereth wore eventually acquired because the depth was not physical but structural, the accumulated weight of three centuries of watching pressing outward through whatever iris was available.

The fisherman was male. Sereth did not mind. She had been men and women and things that had no word in any human language. The body was a costume; the self beneath it had chosen its name long ago.

She opened the new eye. The hilltop looked different through it — the colors shifted, the light recalibrated by the new body's optics. The fisherman's eye was accustomed to water-glare and horizon-distances, and it processed the Ming landscape with a slight strangeness, the way a word from one language sounds odd when spoken with the accent of another.

Sereth stood. The fisherman's body was lighter than the old woman's — less mass, different center of gravity, the joints looser but the muscles thinner. She would need to adjust. She would have time. The Sea was weeks away on foot, and Sereth would use the walking to settle into the new shape the way you settle into a new house: room by room, corner by corner, learning which floorboards creaked and which doors stuck and where the light fell in the afternoon.

She looked down at the monastery. In the courtyard, barely visible through the thinning mist, a monk was crossing toward the central hall. He would find, within the hour, a piece of parchment that had been left on a meditation bench — folded, unsigned, the paper old, the ink faded to the particular brown of writing that had been done decades ago. A map. Hand-drawn, detailed, showing a route through the Canopy's southern edge to the Core shaft, and from there through the underground passages to an exit point on the Sea's western coast. The route was safe — or as safe as any route could be in a world where the Mrak Patrol moved through every region and the Flash Rat's stone-fused skull could feel the memory of magic in the earth from fifty miles away. The map marked the places where the stone's density would mask a traveler's signature, the underground stretches where the Core's mineral weight would absorb the noise, the coastal approach where the Sea's vast memory would swallow the boy's growing chorus the way the lake had swallowed his voice.

The map was old. The handwriting on it belonged to no living person. It belonged to a Canopy cartographer who had died eighty years ago, a woman named Tira who had spent her life mapping the secret passages between regions and who had given her maps to a one-eyed traveling merchant in exchange for news of her daughter in Bahar. Sereth had kept the maps. Sereth kept everything. Three centuries of small transactions, small kindnesses, small exchanges conducted under borrowed faces — the accumulated toolkit of a god-turned-spy who had learned that the most powerful weapon in the world was not magic but information, and that information moved best when carried by someone nobody remembered meeting.

The monk would find the map. The monk would bring it to the elder woman. The elder woman would understand — because the Ming always understood — and she would ensure that it reached the guardian's hands before the boy and the man were beyond the monastery's network of whispered messages and shared knowing.

Sereth turned south.

The Sea was far. Laine was there — Essa's great-great-granddaughter, though the lineage had twisted through enough generations that the blood connection was more memory than biology. Laine did not know about the connection. She did not know about Sereth. She knew only that she was a Guide who could see the trajectory of the future and that every trajectory she followed ended in grief, and that this had made her bitter and funny and sharp and unwilling to believe in prophecies because prophecies, in her experience, were just grief with better marketing.

Sereth needed to reach her first. Needed to prepare the ground. Not with explanations — Laine would not accept explanations. With a letter. A single sentence, left in a place Laine would find it, written in a hand she would not recognize but in a tone she would trust because the tone carried the specific quality of someone who understood grief and was asking her to carry more of it anyway.

_He is worth the grief._

Four words. Sereth had been composing them for fifteen years — since the night the nursery burned and the guardian ran and the last thread of Fletcher's blood disappeared into the Canopy's darkness. Fifteen years of watching from hilltops and trading rumors and redirecting patrols, and the four words had been taking shape beneath all of it, the way a river shapes a stone: slowly, by persistence, until the thing that remains is smooth and essential.

She walked south. The fisherman's legs carried her — longer stride than the old woman's, the gait rolling slightly, the body remembering decades of decks tilting beneath it even though the decades were Sereth's invention and the decks had never existed. The details mattered. The details were what made the performance survive scrutiny — not the face or the voice or the broad strokes of the disguise, but the small, accumulated specificities of a life lived in a particular body, the calluses in the right places, the squint against the right kind of light, the muscles shaped by the right kind of labor.

Behind her, the Ming monastery settled back into its mist. The lake held its memory. The Elder Stone sang beneath the bedrock, sustained and luminous, patient in the way that only things made of sacrifice can be patient — endlessly, without complaint, because the singing was not a task but an identity, and the stone did not know how to be anything other than what it was.

Ahead: the southern hills, the trade roads, the long descent toward the coast. Weeks of walking. Weeks of wearing a new face and settling into a new body and thinking about a boy who was learning to listen and a man who was learning to hope and a young woman on the coast who did not yet know that she was about to be asked to carry the weight of a prophecy she didn't believe in.

_I collect orphans,_ Sereth thought, _the way other people collect regrets. Perhaps they are the same thing._

The thought was not self-pity. It was inventory. Three hundred years of watching over the lost children of a gift she had given — Fletcher's descendants, each one an orphan of the Song, each one born into a world that wanted them dead because of what they might become. Sereth had watched them all. Had guided some. Had failed most. Had buried the knowledge of those failures in the deep strata of a memory that was still divine enough to carry the weight without collapsing, though the weight grew heavier with each generation, and the divinity grew thinner.

This one was different. Aelo was different. Not because his power was greater — though it was, the boy's signature already brighter than his mother's had been at twice his age. Because of the way he carried it. The boy listened. In a world of speakers and shouters and those who used magic like a fist, the boy _listened_. He had been trained to listen by fifteen years of silence and herbs and the slow, devastating kindness of a man who had given up his name and his face and his sobriety to keep one small life burning in the dark.

Perhaps that was enough. Perhaps listening was the thing that would make the difference — the variable that had been missing from every previous attempt, every failed Singer, every descendant who had reached for the Song and been consumed because they approached it as a performance rather than a conversation.

Perhaps. Sereth was not certain. Three hundred years had taught her that certainty was a luxury she could not afford, and that hope was a discipline rather than a feeling — something you practiced, daily, the way the Ming practiced stillness, not because you believed it would work but because the alternative was to stop practicing.

She walked. The hills rose. The road bent south. Behind her, a boy held a stone and listened to the world, and the world, for reasons it could not articulate and did not fully understand, listened back.

Sereth kept walking. She had a letter to deliver.
