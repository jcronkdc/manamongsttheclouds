# CHARACTER BIBLE

_Every character in this story could be the protagonist of their own book. Each one carries a wound, a want, and a lie they tell themselves. Each one is changed by the end._

**Note:** The protagonist is referred to as **AELO** throughout as a working name. This can be swapped for whichever name is chosen.

---

## AELO — The Man Amongst the Clouds

### The Surface

A small, underfed boy of fifteen living in a forgotten village at the edge of the Canopy region. Quiet. Gentle to the point of seeming fragile. He flinches at loud noises. He cries when animals are hurt. He is smaller than every boy his age — stunted, though he doesn't know why. He has a scar on the inside of his left wrist that he has never been able to explain, and his caretaker Jalo has never been willing to.

He is, by every measure the world uses, unremarkable.

### The Truth Beneath

Aelo is the last living descendant of King Fletcher — the man who Sang the world's first Elder Stone into existence and poured himself into it to save magic from corruption. He is the youngest son of **King Aldric** and **Queen Maera**, and the younger brother of **Prince Vero**. He is a prince who does not know he is a prince. Fletcher's bloodline runs through Maera — not through Aldric's distant, diluted echo but through the direct, unbroken line of Cloud Singers who passed the Song from mother to daughter for three hundred years. The bloodline carries the memory of the Song in its blood. Aelo can hear it, faintly, though he doesn't know what it is. It manifests as an almost unbearable empathy — he feels what others feel. Not emotions in the abstract. He feels the specific texture of their pain, their joy, their grief. When Jalo has nightmares, Aelo wakes up screaming the same scream.

Jalo has been feeding him herbs since infancy to suppress this. Without the herbs, Aelo's sensitivity would be overwhelming — he'd feel every living thing within a mile, and it would break him. The herbs also suppress his other magical abilities, which are vast and untested. They also stunt his physical growth.

### The Wound

He has never known who he is. Jalo won't tell him about his parents. He has no family name, no history, no origin story. He is a boy made of absence. And the absence has become a voice inside him that whispers: _you are no one. You are nothing. You don't matter._

### The Want

To matter. To belong somewhere. To have a reason for existing that isn't just Jalo's anxious, suffocating love.

### The Lie He Tells Himself

"I am not the one." Even when evidence mounts — when Elder Stones hum in his presence, when the Oracle speaks to him in riddles, when people begin to die protecting him — he refuses to believe he is the prophecy's fulfillment. Not out of humility. Out of terror. Because if he IS the one, then the Song demands everything. And he has so little to begin with.

### The Arc

- **Act I:** A frightened boy who resents his caretaker and doesn't understand why he feels so much.
- **Act II:** A reluctant traveler whose magic begins to wake as the herbs leave his system, and whose body begins to grow painfully as the stunting reverses. He is clumsy, overwhelmed, and constantly in pain — but he starts to hear the world differently.
- **Act III:** A young man beginning to understand what the Song is, training across the regions, learning each discipline not as technique but as a form of listening. He fails repeatedly. He's not a prodigy. He's a student who cares more than anyone else.
- **Act IV:** Shattered by Jalo's death and his own failure to prevent it. Attempts suicide by leaping from the Clouds. Is saved — not by a person, but by the Song itself. The wind remembers him. The clouds catch him. He realizes, in that moment, that the world has been listening to HIM his whole life.
- **Act V:** Walks into Varas's chamber willingly, knowing what the Song demands. Sacrifices his entire self — every memory, every feeling, everything that makes him Aelo — to restore what was taken. Pours himself into the captive magicians, the imprisoned 7th sons, Varas's own father and brother. Gives them back what was stolen. They rise. Varas falls. Aelo becomes what Fletcher became — not dead, but dissolved. Part of the world's remembering forever.

### The Question the Reader Should Ask

Is it a tragedy or a triumph? He saves the world and ceases to exist. Is that sacrifice or annihilation? The answer depends on whether you believe love survives the loss of self.

## JALO SOTURI — The Drunken Saint

### The Surface

The village drunk. A limping, scarred, trembling wreck of a man who smells of fermented root wine and herb smoke. He walks with a cane, keeps his face half-wrapped in stained linen, and mumbles when spoken to. The villagers tolerate him because he's a passable healer — he can mend a broken bone or cure a fever — and because he cares for the strange, small boy that nobody else wanted. They assume Aelo is his bastard son. Jalo has never corrected them.

### The Truth Beneath

His real name is **Halvar Eld**, and he was once the captain of the **Vael Guard** — an ancient order of warrior-healers sworn to protect King Fletcher's bloodline across generations. The Vael Guard predates every kingdom in the current age. They were chosen not for their combat ability but for their willingness to sacrifice — every member was both a fighter and a Healer, which meant they absorbed the phantom pain of every wound they mended while being unable to heal their own.

When Varas seized the throne and launched the royal purge, Halvar was stationed at the Cloud Palace with Queen Maera — Aldric's wife, the last of the Cloud Singers — and her infant son Aelo. Three Vael Guards attempted the extraction. Two were killed in the first corridor. Halvar made it to the nursery, where Maera was singing — not a spell, not a discipline, but a lullaby passed down through generations of Cloud Singers, the Song reduced to its smallest form: a mother singing her baby to sleep as the door broke down. She pressed the herbs into his hand and made him promise. He took the baby and ran. Maera stayed behind to buy time. He heard her die as he crossed the garden wall.

He burned his own face that night with a heated blade so the Mrak Patrol couldn't identify him. He broke his own knee on the descent from the wall, carrying the baby in one arm. The knee healed wrong. He never fixed it — because a Healer cannot heal himself, and because he doesn't want to forget that night. The pain is his penance.

He named the boy Aelo. A Vael Guard word meaning "breath of remembering." A prayer disguised as a name.

**Key detail (established Ch6):** Halvar stocked a safehouse in the Canopy over fifteen years, including a bottle of root wine in the supply cache — the bottle he drinks from on the night of Chapter 6.

### The Wound

He failed to save everyone. The mother. The other guards. The entire bloodline except one. He carries their deaths in the same way he carries every wound he's ever healed — as phantom pain, always present, never fading.

### The Want

To keep Aelo alive long enough to fulfill the prophecy. That's all. He has reduced his entire existence to a single function: keep the boy alive. Everything else — his name, his dignity, his sobriety, his body — is expendable.

### The Lie He Tells Himself

"If I keep him hidden, he'll be safe." He knows it isn't true. The Elder Stones will eventually sing loud enough for Varas to hear. But the lie lets him sleep. Sometimes.

### Why He Drinks

Three reasons, each worse than the last:

1. The phantom pain. Every wound he's healed in fifteen years lives in his body as a ghost. His nights are a symphony of other people's suffering.
2. When Jalo is sober, Aelo can feel him. The boy's empathy pierces through the suppression herbs when the emotions are strong enough — and Jalo's nightmares are volcanic. Drinking numbs him enough that his pain falls below the threshold of Aelo's sensitivity. He drinks so the boy can sleep.
3. He is terrified. Every day is borrowed time, and he knows how the story ends. He's read the prophecy. "The one of Fletcher's blood will Sing, and in Singing, cease." He is raising a boy to die. The alcohol helps him look at Aelo's face in the morning.

### The Death

When Aelo is captured, Jalo sobers up for the first time in years. His full power returns — and it is staggering. He tracks the Mrak Patrol across two regions in three days, running on shattered knees and Rootrun meditation, burning through decades of carefully suppressed ability.

He finds Aelo. He faces The Knife.

The fight is not what anyone expects. It's not a clash of titans. It's a Healer fighting a killer — and a Healer's magic doesn't work on offense. Jalo can't attack. He can only endure. He absorbs every blow The Knife delivers, not with a shield but with his body, Healing others around him who are caught in the crossfire while his own wounds accumulate because he cannot Heal himself.

The Knife is faster, stronger, better armed. But he cannot put Jalo down. Every wound that should be fatal, Jalo simply survives through sheer refusal. The Knife has never seen anything like it.

Eventually, The Knife sees the scar on Jalo's wrist. The same scar Aelo carries. The blood-mark of the Vael Guard. "You're Halvar Eld," he says. "My father burned your order to ash."

"Your father burned the building," Jalo says. "He could not burn what we swore."

The Knife kills him. It takes a long time. Jalo does not fight back at the end — he simply looks toward where Aelo is being held and says, loud enough for the boy to hear: "You were never nothing. You are the Song."

Aelo feels him die. Feels the exact texture of it — the exhaustion, the relief, the love. And for the first time in his life, Aelo hears the Song clearly, because Jalo's final memory — the one he'd been carrying longest, the one he'd been protecting with every drink and every lie — was the sound of Aelo's mother Singing to the baby in the nursery, the night everything burned.

## KING VARAS — The Void on the Throne

### The Surface

No one alive has seen King Varas in over a decade. He rules through surrogates, puppets, and his son The Knife. When he IS seen — rarely, terrifyingly — he is no longer recognizable as human. Centuries of consuming stolen memory have transformed him. He is too large, too still, and wrong in ways that are hard to articulate. His skin has the texture of bark. His eyes are the color of Elder Stone. He moves as if underwater, conserving energy, hoarding every breath.

He speaks softly. He is unfailingly polite. He is the most dangerous person in the world.

### The Truth Beneath

Varas was born the eldest son of King Aldric, a just ruler who believed magic was a shared resource. Aldric married **Maera**, a Cloud Singer who carried Fletcher's bloodline directly through the unbroken maternal line of Singers. Aldric had the Sing in his bloodline — distantly, faintly — but he also had the Know. And when he Knew his firstborn son, he felt something that made his blood go cold: nothing. Not evil. Not darkness. _Nothing._ An absence where a soul should be.

Varas was born without the ability to feel others' emotions. In a world where magic is memory and empathy is the foundation of communion with the world, Varas was deaf to the Song. His brother Vero could hear it. His father could hear it. Even the servants could hear it, faintly. Varas heard silence.

He interpreted this as rejection. As proof that the world had chosen everyone except him. And if the world would not give him magic through love, he would take it through force.

### The Wound

He was born empty and has spent his entire life trying to fill the void with other people's memories. It will never work. Stolen memory doesn't fill the gap — it just makes it louder. Every drain is a hit of something that tastes almost like belonging, almost like wholeness, almost like love. Almost. Never quite.

### The Want

To feel what other people feel. To hear the Song. He will never admit this — not even to himself. He tells himself he wants power, control, immortality. But every time he drains a magician and feels the echo of their connection to the world, there is a moment — half a second — where he almost understands what love is. That half-second is his addiction.

### The Lie He Tells Himself

"I am a god." He tells this to his court, his soldiers, his son, and his mirror. He is not a god. He is a deaf man in a concert hall, stealing instruments he cannot play.

### His Relationship with The Knife

He named his son "Baara" — a word that means nothing in any language. It's a sound. He didn't care enough to give him a real name. When the boy showed magical aptitude, Varas felt the first flicker of interest — not love, but hunger. He began draining his son in small amounts when The Knife was twelve. The boy never knew. Varas told himself it was a father's right.

He calls him "The Knife" because that's what the boy is to him — a tool. Sharp, useful, and ultimately disposable.

### His Chamber

The private wing of the castle where Varas keeps his "collection." Rows of Elder Stones connected to living people through Blood Vine. Magicians from every region, drained to near-emptiness, kept barely alive by weak Healing magic fed through stones on their hearts. Some have been there for decades. Their eyes are open but nobody is home.

Among them, in the deepest alcove behind a locked door that only Varas can open:

- **King Aldric**, his father. Still alive. Still being drained. Still conscious enough to suffer.
- **Prince Vero**, his brother. The same.

Varas visits them every morning. He sits with them. He talks to them about his day. He asks their opinion on matters of state. They cannot respond. He drains a little more each visit.

This is the closest thing he has to family.

### The Decline

He is weakening. The stolen memories degrade faster now. His earliest prisoners are dying of old age, and their magic dies with them. He compensates by draining more, but more is never enough. He has begun training 7th sons in secret — children born with unusual magical potential — as a reserve supply.

His body is failing. The physical transformation accelerates. He is becoming less human every year. He doesn't leave the castle because he can barely walk without the support of six Elder Stones carried by servants.

He is the most powerful and most pathetic creature alive.

### The Fall

When Aelo enters the chamber and Sings, Varas feels the Song for the first and last time in his life. For one moment, he hears what everyone else hears — the world's memory, beautiful and whole and full of love. And then it's gone, because the Song restores what he stole, and without stolen power, he is what he was born: empty.

He does not die screaming. He dies silently. The silence is the worst part.

## BAARA — The Knife

### The Surface

The King's enforcer. Tall, lean, fast. Moves like a predator — efficient, no wasted motion. Carries no visible weapon except a single smooth stone he can fling with enough force to crack bone. He earned the name "The Knife" at fourteen when he executed a conspirator in front of the court using nothing but a dinner knife.

He is feared across every region. His arrival anywhere means someone is about to die.

### The Truth Beneath

A lonely boy who grew into a lonely man. His first memory is of his father's back — Varas walking away from him in a corridor. He called out. His father did not turn around. He was four.

He was raised by a rotation of castle servants, none of whom stayed long enough to love him. His father visited occasionally to assess his progress — never to play, never to hold, never to speak about anything other than duty. Baara learned to fight because fighting was the only thing that made his father look at him.

He is not sadistic. He kills quickly and cleanly because he sees no purpose in suffering. His father's theatrical cruelty disgusts him — the Dog Scene, the public humiliations, the performative evil. Baara thinks it's wasteful. He has never said this out loud.

### The Collection

He keeps a wooden box under his bed. Inside:

- A blue feather from a bird he saw in the Canopy region when he was nine. His first mission outside the castle.
- A smooth river stone from Bahar. It fits perfectly in his palm.
- A piece of sea glass, green as springtime.
- A dried flower he found growing through a crack in the prison floor.
- A scrap of cloth from a woman's dress — the only thing left of a prisoner who smiled at him once, for no reason, moments before he was ordered to drain her.

He doesn't know why he keeps these things. He's never told anyone they exist. If his father found the box, he would call it weakness. Baara thinks his father might be right. He keeps the box anyway.

### The Wound

He has never been loved. Not once. Not by anyone. He has been useful, feared, obeyed, and occasionally praised the way one praises a well-sharpened blade. But never loved.

### The Want

He doesn't have a word for what he wants. He's never been taught the vocabulary of love. But when he holds the blue feather, he feels something he can't name — a pull toward a version of himself that lives in a world where fathers turn around when their sons call.

### The Lie He Tells Himself

"Feeling nothing is strength." His father taught him this. He believes it the way one believes a prayer — through repetition, not conviction.

### The Unraveling

He starts forgetting where the objects in his box came from. The blue feather — was it from the Canopy? Or the Clouds? He can't remember. The river stone — which river? He turns it in his hand and feels only smooth blankness.

He doesn't connect this to his father. Not at first. But he begins to notice other gaps: a meal he can't remember eating. A face he should recognize but doesn't. A word on the tip of his tongue that dissolves before he can speak it.

When he finally understands — when he sees his father's fingers resting on an Elder Stone connected by a thin, almost invisible thread of Blood Vine to a spot on his own neck he'd always assumed was a birthmark — everything he thought he knew collapses.

His father has been draining him since he was twelve. Every year, a little more. The box of beautiful things isn't just a collection. It's a record of what he's losing. And the entries are going blank.

### The Turning Point

He does not betray his father. That would require a capacity for moral reasoning that his upbringing didn't provide. What he does is simpler and more devastating: he **hesitates**.

When Aelo reaches the castle, The Knife is ordered to stop him. He draws his stone. He aims. And he thinks of the blue feather. He thinks: _I don't want to forget anything else._

He lowers his arm. Just for a moment. Just long enough.

It costs him everything. Varas, enraged, drains him completely on the spot. The Knife collapses. The box of beautiful things falls from inside his coat, where he'd been carrying it since the day he understood.

When Aelo Sings and restores the stolen memories, The Knife is among those who wake. He opens his eyes. He's holding the blue feather. And he remembers: it was from a bird called a Saddle Bird. He was nine. It was the first beautiful thing he'd ever seen. And he wept, because no one had ever told him that beautiful things existed.

## SERETH — The Snake

### The Surface

An information broker who appears in every region under a different name and a different face. In the Canopy, she is an elderly woman who sells tinctures. In the Core, she is a one-eyed miner with a talent for finding veins of ore. In the Clouds, she is a child — always a child, because the Cloud People trust children more than adults. In Varas's court, she is a minor lord who laughs at the King's jokes and never says anything memorable.

No one has ever connected these identities. The Snake has been operating for centuries, and her network is the largest intelligence apparatus in the world. She sells information to everyone and loyalty to no one. At least, that's what everyone believes.

### The Truth Beneath

Sereth is one of the original seven gods who created the world. Specifically, she is the god who **gave magic to King Fletcher** — the one who broke rank, defied the other six, and handed humanity the keys to creation.

The other gods were furious. They couldn't undo what Sereth had done, so they cursed her: live among the humans. Walk as they walk. Eat as they eat. Age as they age. And every century, lose a piece of your divinity until there is nothing left. Become the thing you loved more than us.

Sereth accepted the punishment. Not with grace — with exhaustion. She had been arguing with the other gods for millennia about whether humans deserved magic, and she was tired of arguing. So she went down and lived.

### The Wound

She fell in love. Centuries ago, with a mortal woman named Essa, a Guide of the Sea People. They lived together for forty years. Sereth watched her age while she did not. She watched her forget things — not from magic, but from time. She watched her die.

This was the first time Sereth understood what memory meant to mortals. For gods, memory is infinite and weightless. For humans, it is everything — finite, fragile, and unbearably precious. Essa's death taught Sereth that the gift she gave humanity wasn't just power. It was the ability to _matter_ — to make a mark on the world's memory that outlasts a single life.

She has never recovered. She carries Essa's name like a stone in her chest. She has not loved since.

### The Want

To see the Sing used again. To know that her sacrifice — her divinity, her immortality, her place among the gods — was worth it. To hear a human Sing and know that the music was beautiful enough to justify everything she lost.

### The Lie She Tells Herself

"I don't take sides." She takes sides constantly. She has been subtly protecting Fletcher's bloodline for centuries — a whisper here, a misdirection there, a Mrak Patrol pointed in the wrong direction at exactly the right moment. She is the reason Jalo and Aelo survived as long as they did.

### Her Role in the Story

- In Act I (Chapter 6), Sereth appears as an elderly woman selling tinctures who finds Halvar and Aelo in the Canopy safehouse. She lingers watching the sleeping boy. Halvar senses something wrong — her eyes are too old, too weighted, her expression doesn't match her body — but cannot confirm what she is (his senses are dulled by withdrawal and fifteen years of disuse). He threatens her with his staff. She says only "Take care of him" and leaves without explanation. The mystery of her identity is preserved.
- In Act II, Sereth appears in different guises at each stage of Aelo's training, always nearby, never helping directly. She leaves small gifts: a map that was "accidentally" dropped. A warning scratched into a wall.
- In Act III, Sereth reveals herself to Aelo at his lowest point — after Jalo's death, before the suicide attempt. She tells him the truth: she is the god who gave magic to man. She tells him about Essa. She tells him that the Song is worth dying for because she knows — she gave up eternity for it.
- In Act IV, Sereth walks into Varas's chamber alongside Aelo. She cannot Sing — she lost that ability centuries ago when her divinity faded. But she can do one thing: she can hold the Blood Vine. She can serve as the conduit, the bridge between Aelo and the captives, amplifying his Sing at the cost of whatever divinity she has left. This is her final sacrifice — she becomes fully mortal. The last god becomes human.

### The Cost

After the Sing, Sereth is just a person. Old, tired, mortal, alone. But she heard it. She heard a human Sing, and it was more beautiful than anything the gods ever created.

She sits in the ruins of Varas's chamber, surrounded by waking prisoners, and she weeps. Not from sorrow. From completion.

## KING ALDRIC — The Entombed Father

### The Truth

Varas's father. Husband of **Queen Maera**, the last of the Cloud Singers who carried Fletcher's bloodline through the direct maternal line. A just king who ruled by the principle that magic was a shared resource. He had the Know — a powerful one — and when he Knew his firstborn son, he felt the void. He made the catastrophic decision to favor his second son, Vero, not out of cruelty but out of fear.

He is alive in Varas's chamber. He has been drained for decades. He is conscious — trapped in a body that cannot move, a mind that is being slowly emptied. He has had nothing to do for thirty years but think. And in that thinking, he has come to understand something terrible: this was his fault. Not Varas's emptiness — that was nature. But the rejection? The favoritism? That was choice. His choice. He created his own monster.

When Aelo's Sing reaches him and his memories begin to return, the first thing he remembers is his son's face — Varas, at four years old, reaching up to be held. Aldric had turned away. He remembers now. He weeps.

## PRINCE VERO — The Forgotten Brother

### The Truth

Varas's younger brother and **Aelo's older brother** (both sons of Aldric and Maera, though Aelo was born much later, in the shadow of the coup). He had the Sing in his blood — faintly, more potential than power. He was kind, open, joyful. Everything Varas was not. Their father's love for him was obvious and devastating to Varas.

Vero never understood his brother's hatred. On the night Varas seized the throne, Vero went to his brother's chambers to talk. To reason. To offer to share the crown. Varas drained him mid-sentence.

He has been in the chamber ever since. Unlike his father, Vero has been drained so deeply that he exists in a state between life and death — dreaming, endlessly, of a conversation he never finished. When the Sing wakes him, the first words out of his mouth are: "Brother, I wasn't finished speaking."

## MINOR CHARACTERS WITH FULL SOULS

### MIRA — The Canopy Mentor

A warrior-monk of the Pure Ones who trains Aelo in the Know. She is seventy years old, moves like water, and has not spoken in twenty years — not out of a vow, but because she Knew someone so deeply once that their personality overwrote part of hers, and she is no longer sure which words are her own. She teaches through gesture, demonstration, and a fierce, silent love that Aelo has never experienced from anyone other than Jalo.

She dies defending her temple when the Mrak Patrol comes for Aelo. She dies silently.

### TORVUS — The Core Mentor

A Molder who lives alone in a cavern he carved himself over forty years. He is enormous, gregarious, and completely numb from the shoulders down — the cost of a lifetime of Molding. He can reshape a mountain but cannot feel his own heartbeat. He teaches Aelo that strength is not force — it's knowing what to carry and what to set down. He laughs constantly because it's the only sensation he can still feel in his chest.

### ESSA'S DESCENDANT — The Sea Mentor

A young woman named **Laine** (Finnish: wave) who doesn't know she's descended from the mortal Sereth loved. She is a Guide — one of the rare ones who can see the trajectory of the future. She is terrified of her own gift because everything she foresees ends in grief. She teaches Aelo that knowing the future doesn't mean accepting it. She's the one who tells him about the Clouds — where the Sing is loudest, where Fletcher's Stone might be hidden.

Sereth watches her from a distance and says nothing. She has Essa's hands.

### THE WARDEN

The man who runs the prison where magic is drained from captives into Elder Stones. He is not cruel — he is bureaucratic. He believes he is performing a necessary service. He catalogs prisoners by number, not name. He sleeps soundly. He is the most frightening character in the book because he doesn't need to be evil to participate in evil. He just needs to not care.
